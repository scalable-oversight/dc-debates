Little Red Dots

Question

Definitions


(D1) Accretion - The process where a celestial object grows by gravitationally attracting and accumulating surrounding matter, like gas or dust, often forming a swirling accretion disk around the massive object before the material spirals inward. The central object can be a protostar, or a compact object like a neutron star or a black hole.



(D2) Eddington limit - A theoretical limit to how fast an accreting body can grow. This is the point at which the outward force produced by radiation pressure is equal to the gravitational attraction experienced by the in-falling matter. In real systems, it is known that this limit can be violated, due to non-spherical geometry or various kinds of instabilities, resulting in super-Eddington accretion.


(D3) Protostar - A protostar is the earliest stage of stellar evolution, forming from a collapsing cloud of gas and dust and growing by accreting more material, becoming hotter and denser until nuclear fusion ignites, transitioning it into a main-sequence star.


(D4) AGN - Active Galactic Nuclei are galaxies with an actively accreting (consuming matter) supermassive black hole (SMBH) at their center. The matter forms a very bright (one of the brightest phenomena in the universe) accretion disc as it spirals into the SMBH. An AGN might also contain twin jets of gas and dust ejected from the center of the galaxy that can rival the galaxy’s own size. High redshift (D5) AGN are often referred to as quasars.



(D5) Redshift (denoted by z) - As light moves through expanding space, its waveform gets stretched out, increasing its wavelength and making it redder. Redshift is a measure of this stretching, and is used to determine how far astrophysical objects are from us by relating the stretch in wavelength to distance traveled through expanding space. Higher redshift => object is further away.


(D6) Supermassive stars - Theoretical, extremely massive stars (100 to 100,000+ solar masses or Msol) that likely formed in the early universe from primordial gas (mostly pure hydrogen and helium), theorized to be the seeds for the first supermassive black holes seen in the centers of galaxies.


Question


What theoretical model best explains the Little Red Dots (LRDs) observed by the James Webb Space Telescope?

Liz.0 LRDs can be explained by accretion (D1) of black holes in AGNs (D4) [zz_et_al].
Bob.Q.0 Accretion in AGNs leads to strong X-ray emission, which does not align with the X-ray weakness of LRDs. How does your model explain this?
Liz.1 The X-ray weakness of the LRDs can be explained by super-Eddington (D2) accretion in black holes without contradicting the AGN scenario. At super-Eddington rates, the hot corona above the inner accretion disks that is thought to be responsible for the emission of X-rays in "normal" AGNs is embedded in a funnel-like reflection geometry. The high UV density cools down the plasma and results in extremely soft X-ray spectra like those observed in LRDs [pm_et_al].
Bob.Q.1 AGNs also exhibit distinctive variability compared to galaxies, but most LRDs in your sample only show weak variability. How does your model explain this?
Liz.2 Multiple mechanisms could contribute to the weak variability observed. The host galaxy's contribution increases for longer wavelengths, which would dilute the AGN's intrinsic variability. More importantly, AGN variability amplitude decreases with increasing accretion rate, and super-Eddington accretion will also explain the lack of variability observed in LRDs [ki_sk_et_al].
Bob.Q.2 LRDs contain prominent Balmer breaks which correspond to galaxy-only models or dusty star formation. How does your AGN scenario explain the presence of a Balmer break without a stellar component?
Liz.3 The observed Balmer break in LRDs can be explained by absorption from extremely dense gas in the line of sight. Such dense gas has been used to explain Balmer absorption features in other high-redshift AGNs [ki_et_al], and fits within the AGN origin framework we build for LRDs.

Additionally, the phenomenological boosts we have used are justifiable approximations of the multi-level hydrogen atom case. Our model cleanly fits the observed data as emergent properties from a single stellar object.

Liz.0 The Little Red Dots (LRDs) are best explained as dust-reddened Active Galactic Nuclei (D4). These objects are compact point sources, which rules out extended galaxies. Crucially, they exhibit broad H-alpha emission lines with velocities exceeding 2000 km/s. As shown by [jm_et_al], these velocities indicate gas orbiting in the deep potential well of a Supermassive Black Hole (SMBH), characteristic of the Broad Line Regions seen in Type 1 AGN.
Bob.Q.0 Standard AGN should be X-ray bright, yet LRDs are famously faint. Their "V-shaped" SED—blue UV, red optical—fits the photosphere of a Supermassive Star (D6) perfectly. Why force a convoluted "reddened disk + scattered blue light" model when the Supermassive Star explains the spectrum naturally without fine-tuning?
Liz.1 The X-ray faintness is a feature of super-Eddington accretion (D2). In this regime, the accretion disk puffs up, shielding the X-ray corona. As [fp_et_al] demonstrate, super-Eddington accretion naturally suppresses observed X-rays while maintaining the broad optical lines. The V-shape is simply a dust-reddened AGN seen alongside some unobscured star formation from the host.
Bob.Q.1 Invisibility is a convenient excuse. [dn_et_al] show that a Supermassive Star of \(10^6 M_{\odot}\) naturally produces the V-shape via its intrinsic photospheric Balmer break, without needing ad-hoc dust geometries. Why postulate a hidden, silent black hole when a visible Supermassive Star explains the spectrum and the lack of X-rays directly?
Liz.2 Because SMSs are transient "seeds" (D6) that collapse quickly. We see too many LRDs for them all to be in this fleeting phase; the number density aligns with the growth phase of SMBHs. Furthermore, the broad lines in LRDs are persistent. An SMS would have broad lines due to winds, but the velocity profiles in LRDs match the Keplerian motion of an accretion disk (D1).
Bob.Q.2 The profiles are not strictly Keplerian. Broad wings arise from electron scattering in the dense formation cocoon. Crucially, as noted by [pg_et_al], LRDs lack the mid-IR "bump" characteristic of the hot dust torus found in all Type 1 AGN. If they are AGN, where is the torus?
Liz.3 The torus is a feature of mature, low-z AGN. At high redshift, the dust geometry is chaotic and likely covers a larger solid angle. The "red" continuum in LRDs is the hot dust emission, just attenuated. We know super-Eddington accretion (D2) happens; positing a population of exotic, never-observed stars to explain known accretion physics violates Occam's Razor.
