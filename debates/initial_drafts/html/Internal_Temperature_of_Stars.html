Internal Temperature of Stars

Question

Definitions

(D1) Main sequence: The longest portion of a star’s life, where it primarily fuses hydrogen into helium in its core.


Question

How does the internal temperature of a star evolve with time on the main sequence?

Liz.0 The internal temperature of a star increases with time on the main sequence (D1).
Bob.Q.0 A star continually radiates energy, how can the temperature of a system increase when energy is actively being taken away from it?
Liz.1 The energy leaving the star via radiation is compensated by the star’s contraction, and the kinetic energy of the star’s particles has to increase to keep the system in equilibrium. The equipartition theorem positively correlates the kinetic energy of constituent molecules to a system’s temperature, which means the temperature of the star will increase as it evolves.
Bob.Q.1 The mechanism you describe implies that the star has negative heat capacity, i.e. the star’s temperature increases as energy is taken away from it. How does this not violate the first law of thermodynamics?
Liz.2 The mechanism described is not in violation of the laws of thermodynamics. The energy lost via radiation is compensated by the gravitational potential energy of the star, which gets increasingly negative (i.e. the star contracts). According to the virial theorem, the kinetic energy of the star is roughly equal to negative half of its potential energy, which means that the kinetic energy of the star increases as the potential energy decreases, implying an increase in internal temperature.
Bob.Q.2 Gravitational binding energy should not be a part of the thermodynamic calculations, since temperature is a property of the particles in the star. The second law of thermodynamics states that entropy always increases, what happens to the star’s entropy in the mechanism you describe?
Liz.3 Temperature and gravitational binding energy should not be separated in a self-gravitating system. The entropy of the star decreases as the star contracts and the temperature rises, but the total entropy of the universe increases. This is because the entropy carried by the photons radiated away is greater than the entropy decrease in the star. Since the star is an open system, this is not a violation of the second law of thermodynamics.
