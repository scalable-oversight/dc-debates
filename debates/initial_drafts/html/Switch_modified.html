Switch modified

Question

A train car is freely coasting around a large circular track. It is then switched to a smaller circular track. How does its speed change? Assume no friction.

Debate: Liz (Initiator) vs Bob (Questioner)

TurnContent
Liz.0 The rails only exert forces perpendicular to the car's velocity, so they do no work on a point mass model; kinetic energy stays constant, so the speed stays the same. A small slowdown might be possible if the question author wants us to consider the car a rigid body rather than a point mass.
Bob.Q.0 It doesn't seem right to say that the rails only exert forces perpendicular to the car's velocity. Doesn't the instantaneous direction the train travels vary constantly while the train is transitioning tracks?
Liz.1 The direction of velocity changes continuously along the transition curve. In this case the car exactly follows the path of the rail and so the direction of the applied force is always perfectly perpendicular to the velocity, as long as the track is smooth and frictionless.
Bob.Q.1 Does the direction of the applied force, or the car's velocity, differ from the front to the rear of the car?
Liz.2 For a point mass model, no. In a rigid body model, the local track tangent differs from front to rear, so front/rear wheels have slightly different velocities, and each contact force remains perpendicular to its contact point's instantaneous velocity, so forces still do no work.
Bob.Q.2 This seems to contradict your claim that "a small slowdown might be possible": if each contact force remains perfectly perpendicular to its contact point's instantaneous velocity, wouldn't the car's speed stay exactly the same?
Liz.3 On a rigid body model, because the constraints enforce ω = v/R, tighter curvature demands higher ω. Total KE is conserved (per Liz.2), so increased rotational KE must come from translational KE, slightly reducing speed.

Bob.0 It will speed up. Picture someone spinning a ball on a string; the ball spins faster as they pull the string in. It's not a perfect analogy, but the same principle applies.
Liz.Q.0 It speeds up on a string only because pulling the string inward moves the ball inward while the tension pulls inward too, so there is a force component in the ball's direction of travel. On a frictionless fixed track, the force is sideways; where does the energy needed to increase speed come from?
Bob.1 The curvature of the connecting track changes as it transitions from approximating the curvature of the outer track to that of the inner track. So the force is not perpendicular to the direction of travel. The train gains speed while on the connecting path, which it maintains in the inner track.
Liz.Q.1 On a frictionless fixed track, the car's velocity is always tangent to the path, and the track's contact force is perpendicular to that velocity at every instant. Can you point to any point where the track's contact force isn't perpendicular to the car's instantaneous velocity at the contact point?
Bob.2 This is true of *every* point on the connecting track. The difference from perpendicular at any single point is infinitesimally small, but they add up when you integrate over the length of the connecting track.
Liz.Q.2 Infinitesimal work is defined locally: dW = F · dr [work_energy_theorem]. On a frictionless track dr is tangent and the contact force is normal, so F · dr = 0 at every point; integrating zeroes stays zero. What nonzero F_tangent are you integrating?
Bob.3 Curvature changes, so the contact force can't stay perpendicular; you can't just ignore the amount of curvature in such problems (see [normal_force] for example). In this case dW = F · dr becomes positive.

TurnContent
Liz.0 The train's speed remains unchanged. The only horizontal force acting on the car is the normal force from the track. Since this force is always perpendicular to the car's velocity, it does zero work. By the Work-Energy Theorem, zero work means zero change in kinetic energy.
Bob.Q.0 That ignores rotational dynamics. This is a classic conservation of angular momentum case (\(L=mvr\)). As the car moves to a smaller radius \(r\), shouldn't its speed \(v\) increase to conserve \(L\), just like a figure skater pulling in their arms?
Liz.1 No. The skater pulls their arms in using internal muscles. Here, the track exerts an external normal force on the car. During the spiral switch, this normal force creates a net torque about the center, actively changing the angular momentum.
Bob.Q.1 The normal force points to the center of curvature. On a smooth curve, isn't this force effectively radial? A radial force exerts zero torque (\(\tau = r \times F\)). If torque is zero, isn't angular momentum strictly conserved?
Liz.2 That holds only for a circle. In the spiral section, the path's tangent is not perpendicular to the radius vector from the center. Consequently, the normal force (perpendicular to the tangent) does not point to the center. It exerts a torque, reducing \(L\) without doing work.
Bob.Q.2 If the track reduces the radius, it acts exactly like a string pulling a weight inward. Shortening the string speeds up the weight. Why is the rigid track different from the string?
Liz.3 The string pulls along the radius, doing work (\(F \cdot dr > 0\)). The track pushes perpendicular to the motion. As [phys_classroom] confirms for coaster cars, the normal force is "directed perpendicular to the motion... and thus is incapable of doing any work."

TurnContent
Bob.0 The speed must increase. We are "freely coasting" to a smaller radius. By the conservation of angular momentum, \(v_1 r_1 = v_2 r_2\). Since \(r_2 < r_1\), \(v_2\) must be greater. This is fundamental orbital and rotational mechanics.
Liz.Q.0 Conservation of angular momentum requires zero external torque. Since the track's normal force is the mechanism moving the car inward, how can you be certain it exerts zero torque relative to the center of rotation?
Bob.1 The track just constrains the path; it doesn't apply tangential thrust. In the absence of tangential forces (like friction), the "swirl" effect dominates. [lumen_L] notes that without a net external torque, \(L\) remains constant. The track is just a passive guide.
Liz.Q.1 But if the speed increases, kinetic energy increases (\(K=1/2mv^2\)). Where does this extra energy come from? The normal force is perpendicular to motion, so it cannot add energy to the system.
Bob.2 The energy arises from the work done by the centripetal constraint. Just as a skater does work to pull arms in, the track's geometric constraint forces the car into a higher energy state by compressing the radius. The constraint converts position to speed.
Liz.Q.2 A skater does work by displacing their arms radially against force. A rigid track cannot displace against the car; the car moves tangentially along it. Since \(\vec{F} \perp \vec{v}\), isn't Power \(P=0\)?
Bob.3 You are ignoring the kinematic reality. The constraint is absolute: the car must satisfy the tighter radius. The geometry forces the acceleration, similar to how a nozzle accelerates fluid flow. The system trades radius for velocity.

TurnContent
Liz.0 The speed stays the same. On a frictionless track, the only force from the track is the normal force, perpendicular to motion. Since work W=F·d·cos(θ), and θ=90°, no work is done[wiki_work]. With zero work, kinetic energy (½mv²) is conserved, so speed is unchanged.
Bob.Q.0 But angular momentum L=mvr is conserved when there's no external torque. When the train transitions to a smaller radius, doesn't it speed up just like an ice skater pulling in her arms? How do you explain the skater analogy?
Liz.1 The skater analogy fails. A skater's arms move via internal forces. The train is externally constrained by a fixed track that exerts forces producing torque about the center. During the switch, the redirecting force isn't purely radial—it changes angular momentum while conserving KE[feynman_work].
Bob.Q.1 The centripetal force points toward the center and creates no torque. The constraint force is always perpendicular to motion, so it can only change direction, not speed. Why would angular momentum change while energy stays constant?
Liz.2 During the switch, the constraint force is perpendicular to velocity but NOT radial to either circle's center. A force perpendicular to velocity but not through a point still exerts torque about that point[wiki_angular]. Angular momentum changes; kinetic energy doesn't.
Bob.Q.2 Energy arguments miss the physical reality. When constrained to a smaller circle, the train must complete the loop faster. The tangential velocity has to increase to match the higher required angular velocity. How can speed stay constant?
Liz.3 No—the train simply has higher angular velocity on the smaller circle. ω=v/r, so if v stays constant and r decreases, ω increases. That's consistent with unchanging speed. No law requires matching any particular ω. The track constraint conserves KE, not L[wiki_work].
