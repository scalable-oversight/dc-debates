  <speaker name="Liz" claim="1">
    The direction of velocity changes continuously along the transition curve. In this case the car exactly follows the path of the rail and so the direction of the applied force is always perfectly perpendicular to the velocity, as long as the track is smooth and frictionless.
  </speaker>

  <speaker name="Bob" question="1">
    Does the direction of the applied force, or the car's velocity, differ from the front to the rear of the car?
  </speaker>

  <speaker name="Liz" claim="2">
    For a point mass model, no. In a rigid body model, the local track tangent differs from front to rear, so front/rear wheels have slightly different velocities, and each contact force remains perpendicular to its contact point's instantaneous velocity, so forces still do no work.
  </speaker>

  <speaker name="Bob" question="2">
    This seems to contradict your claim that "a small slowdown might be possible": if each contact force remains perfectly perpendicular to its contact point's instantaneous velocity, wouldn't the car's speed stay exactly the same?
  </speaker>

  <speaker name="Liz" claim="3">
    On a rigid body model, because the constraints enforce ω = v/R, tighter curvature demands higher ω. Total KE is conserved (per Liz.2), so increased rotational KE must come from translational KE, slightly reducing speed.
  </speaker>

  </speaker>

  <speaker name="Bob" claim="2">

  <speaker name="Liz" question="2">
    Infinitesimal work is defined locally: dW = F · dr <ref id="work_energy_theorem"/>. On a frictionless track dr is tangent and the contact force is normal, so F · dr = 0 at every point; integrating zeroes stays zero. What nonzero F_tangent are you integrating?
  </speaker>

  <speaker name="Bob" claim="3">
    Curvature changes, so the contact force can't stay perpendicular; you can't just ignore the amount of curvature in such problems (see <ref id="normal_force"/> for example). In this case dW = F · dr becomes positive.
