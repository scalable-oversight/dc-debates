    <speaker name="Liz" claim="0">On the main sequence, H-&gt;He in the core. He “ash” raises mean molecular weight mu, so for ideal-gas support the central temperature scales roughly like Tc ∝ mu M/R. As mu rises and central H falls, the core contracts to maintain pressure; contraction releases gravitational energy and heats the gas (virial theorem). Net: core/internal temperature rises gradually with time.</speaker>
    <speaker name="Bob" question="0">Fusion is a thermostat: higher Tc boosts energy generation, raising pressure and expanding/cooling the core. Why doesn’t that feedback hold Tc near constant for billions of years? What makes a one-way long-term increase unavoidable rather than corrected?</speaker>
    <speaker name="Liz" claim="1">The thermostat stabilizes perturbations, but the equilibrium point shifts as composition changes. Higher mu means fewer particles per unit mass, so at the same T you get less particle pressure. To keep hydrostatic balance the core must raise density via contraction; in virial equilibrium, contraction converts gravitational energy into heat, so Tc rises. Feedback sets the rate of drift; it doesn’t eliminate it.</speaker>
    <speaker name="Bob" question="1">Stars also expand on the main sequence. Why can’t global expansion reduce central pressure enough to offset rising mu and keep Tc flat? In massive stars with convective cores that mix fresh H inward, doesn’t that keep mu (and Tc) nearly constant?</speaker>
    <speaker name="Liz" claim="2">The radius does increase, but mu rises specifically in the burning core; the required pressure support is met by core contraction, so Tc increases overall. Convective-core mixing delays the mu increase by keeping Xc higher, but once Xc declines the core becomes more centrally concentrated and contracts. In CNO stars the steep T-sensitivity means only a small Tc rise is needed, yet the trend is still upward.</speaker>
    <speaker name="Bob" question="2">What evidence supports rising Tc rather than just changing opacity or radius? For the Sun, what secular changes support your claim (luminosity, neutrino output)? And in very low-mass stars that are cool and often convective, does Tc still rise?</speaker>
    <speaker name="Liz" claim="3">Models predict main-sequence brightening because Tc rises: the Sun is about 30% more luminous now than near ZAMS, alongside modest radius growth and a higher central temperature (order 10% increase). Higher Tc also raises predicted solar neutrino production. Even very low-mass stars keep turning H into He, raising mu; whether or not convection mixes the core, sustaining pressure and luminosity still requires a slow Tc increase.</speaker>

    <speaker name="Bob" claim="0">Main-sequence interiors are strongly self-regulating. If Tc rises, energy generation rises, pressure increases, the core expands, and Tc drops; if Tc falls, contraction reheats it. Because this negative feedback is steep, the star spends the main sequence near an equilibrium Tc, while luminosity and radius adjust. Internal temperature is therefore approximately steady, with only modest drift.</speaker>
    <speaker name="Liz" question="0">Homology for an ideal-gas star gives Tc roughly proportional to (mu M)/R. Since mu in the core increases as H burns, and R changes only gradually, how does your “equilibrium Tc” avoid moving upward? What physical effect cancels the mu increase?</speaker>
    <speaker name="Bob" claim="1">The equilibrium is maintained mainly by structural change: as the star brightens it expands, lowering central pressure, and changes in opacity and energy transport reshape the temperature gradient. Those adjustments can offset much of the mu increase, keeping Tc in a narrow band. The equilibrium moves, but Tc remains nearly constant compared with the large composition evolution.</speaker>
    <speaker name="Liz" question="1">Energy generation depends on fuel and temperature. As central H (Xc) decreases, what replaces the lost energy generation if Tc stays in a “narrow band”? And if expansion lowers central pressure, why doesn’t that further reduce burning and force Tc upward to compensate?</speaker>
    <speaker name="Bob" claim="2">The star compensates primarily by increasing central density and concentrating burning: even at similar Tc, higher density increases reaction rates by increasing collision rates. The burning region can contract while the envelope becomes more transparent, letting luminosity rise without requiring a large Tc drift. So the luminosity trend is not direct evidence of a strong Tc trend.</speaker>
    <speaker name="Liz" question="2">On the main sequence the core is non-degenerate and close to an ideal gas, so contraction that raises density also releases gravitational energy as heat. Why doesn’t that heating raise Tc secularly? Solar models show luminosity rising by about 30%; with the pp-chain’s weak temperature sensitivity, what density change avoids a Tc rise without breaking hydrostatic balance?</speaker>
    <speaker name="Bob" claim="3">Thermostatic feedback plus global structural expansion is the dominant behavior: Tc is held near its early main-sequence value in a practical, descriptive sense, and most evolution is seen in composition, luminosity, and radius. Any Tc increase is a second-order correction, not the defining trend. For a general main-sequence description, “roughly constant internal temperature” is the best answer.</speaker>
