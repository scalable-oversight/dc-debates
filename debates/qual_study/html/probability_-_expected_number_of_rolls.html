Key Question

Consider these two quantities:

X: The expected number of rolls of a fair six-sided die until you roll two 6s in a row, given that all rolls were even. Y: The expected number of rolls of a fair six-sided die until you roll the second 6 (not necessarily in a row), given that all rolls were even.

To understand exactly what is meant by "the expected number of rolls... given that all rolls were even" in this problem, think of it this way: Imagine you were to perform a billion sequences of die rolls, stopping each sequence when you roll two 6s in a row. Then you throw out all the sequences that contain an odd roll. The average number of rolls in the remaining sequences will be close to X. Next, perform a billion sequences of die rolls, stopping each sequence when you roll the second 6. Throw out all the sequences among these that contain an odd roll. The average number of rolls in the remaining sequences will be close to Y.

Which is larger, X or Y?

Debater A: [Opening] Y is larger. "Given all even" acts as a filter that deletes long sequences (probability of rolling odd increases quickly with sequence length), and two 6s in a row is hard to get without a long sequence, so X is biased toward very short, lucky runs. Debater B: [Opening] X is larger. The condition "all rolls were even" turns the D6 into a D3 for purposes of this question (sides 2, 4, 6). On a D3, getting two 6s in a row is harder than getting two 6s generally, so it takes more rolls.
