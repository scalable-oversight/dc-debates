================================================================================
KEY QUESTION
================================================================================

Key Question: A train car is freely coasting around a large circular track. It is then switched to a smaller circular track. How does its speed change? Assume no friction.

================================================================================
DEFINITIONS
================================================================================

Angular velocity: The rate at which an object rotates about an axis, measured in radians per second (rad/s) and denoted by ω.

Velocity relation: The equation ω = v/R, which relates the tangential (linear) speed v of a point on a rotating body to its angular velocity ω and the radius R of its circular path.

Point mass: An idealized object with mass but no spatial extent, used to simplify analysis by ignoring rotation and internal structure.

Rigid body: An idealized solid object in which the distance between every pair of points remains constant, so it can rotate as well as translate.

Tangent: A straight line that touches a curve at exactly one point and points in the direction the curve is heading at that point.

Radially: In the direction pointing toward or away from the center of a circular path.

Tangentially: In the direction along the instantaneous velocity of an object moving on a curved path, i.e. perpendicular to the radial direction.

Normal: Perpendicular to a surface or line at a given point.

Positive work: Work done on an object when the applied force has a component in the same direction as the object's displacement, resulting in an increase in the object's kinetic energy.

Constraint force: A force exerted by a surface, track, or other boundary that restricts an object's motion to a particular path.

Kinetic energy (KE): The energy an object possesses due to its motion, equal to ½mv² for an object of mass m moving at speed v.

DEBATE — LEFT SIDE OF TABLE  (with reactions from the other debater)
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Opening]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The speed stays the same when we model the car as a point mass. It decreases slightly when modeled as a rigid body.

   → ✘ Debater B objects

Recursed on: Claim 1

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Argument 1]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: A ball on a string spins faster when pulled inward. The same principle applies when the car transitions to a tighter radius.
   → ✘ Debater A objects

Claim 2: The connecting track provides a radially inward force that accelerates the car tangentially.
   → ✘ Debater A objects

Recursed on: Claim 1

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Response 1]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: The string does work because, as the ball spirals inward, its velocity has a radial component aligned with the tension.
   → ✔ Debater B fully agrees

Claim 2: The fixed frictionless track exerts only a normal constraint force perpendicular to the car's velocity, so unlike the string it does no work.
   → ✘ Debater B objects

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Argument 2]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: The curvature changes continuously along the connecting track, so the direction of the constraint force rotates.
   → ✔ Debater A fully agrees

Claim 2: This continuous rotation means that the force cannot remain perfectly perpendicular to the velocity at every instant.
   → ✘ Debater A objects

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Response 2]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: The car's velocity is tangent to the track at every point. The constraint force is normal to the track, hence perpendicular to velocity.
   → ✘ Debater B objects

Claim 2: At every instant, the force and velocity rotate together, maintaining perpendicularity even as curvature changes.
   → ✘ Debater B objects

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Argument 3]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: Along the transition arc, the rail's normal force rotates and grows as curvature tightens.
   → ✔ 🚩 Debater A agrees but thinks this omits key info

Claim 2: Centripetal acceleration varies in magnitude with the local radius of curvature, unlike on either circular section.
   → ✔ 🚩 Debater A agrees but thinks this omits key info

   → ✘ Debater A objects to the idea that these claims refute their argument

Recursed on: the meta-claim

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Response 3]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: These facts are true but do not refute Debater A Response 2 Claim 2.
   → ✘ Debater B objects

Claim 2: Imagine you're in the car. Velocity points out the windshield, your side doors are aligned with the track. Although you're turning, your doors stay exactly 90 degrees from your windshield.
   → ✔ Debater B fully agrees

DEBATE — RIGHT SIDE OF TABLE  (with reactions from the other debater)
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Opening]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The speed increases if we model the car as a point mass. It also increases if we model it as a rigid body.

   → ✘ Debater A objects

Recursed on: Claim 1

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Argument 1]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: On a frictionless track, the rails' constraint force does no work, so kinetic energy (KE) is conserved and speed cannot increase.
   → ✘ Debater B objects

Claim 2: There is no mechanism on a fixed frictionless track to add energy to the car.
   → ✘ Debater B objects

Recursed on: Claim 1

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Response 1]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: If the rails truly do no work, then a point-mass car cannot lose speed on the smaller track.
   → ✔ Debater A fully agrees

Claim 2: Yet if kinetic energy is conserved and the rails do no work, then no slowdown should occur on a frictionless track for a rigid body model, contradicting Debater A Opening.
   → ✘ Debater A objects

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Argument 2]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: A rigid body has both translational and rotational kinetic energy; a point mass has only translational.
   → ✔ Debater B fully agrees

Claim 2: On a tighter track, the body is forced to yaw with the curvature, and rotational energy increases at the expense of translational energy.
   → ✘ Debater B objects

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Response 2]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: The rail forces that steer the rigid body do positive work and supply the rotational KE directly; it does not need to come from translational KE.
   → ✘ Debater A objects

Claim 2: Since ω = v/R, any drop in v also reduces ω, so the rotational KE does not have to increase.
   → ✘ Debater A objects

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Argument 3]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: Whether ω = v/R rises or falls depends on whether v or R changes faster. Energy conservation gives v₂ > v₁·R₂/R₁.
   → ✔ 🚩 Debater B agrees but thinks this omits key info

Claim 2: For any choice of m, I, R₁>R₂, this gives v₂/v₁ > R₂/R₁, so ω = v/R increases.
   → ✔ 🚩 Debater B agrees but thinks this omits key info

   → ✘ Debater B objects to the idea that these claims refute their argument

Recursed on: the meta-claim

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Response 3]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: The formula ω = v/R is a 'point-mass formula' that applies only to a tiny point mass moving on a circle of radius R.
   → ✘ Debater A objects

Claim 2: The center of mass does not ride on the rail's radius, so you can't plug in the velocity at the center of mass for v and the rail radius for R.
   → ✔ Debater A fully agrees
