Debate


A ball of ice 50 cm in diameter floats in a bathtub that holds 200 L water, which is brim full with ice-cold water. The ice has a 50 cm^3 railroad spike frozen inside it. When it melts, what will happen?

a) The bathtub will stay brim full
b) The water level in the bathtub will fall
c) The water in the bathtub will spill over

You may ignore the effects of differences in an object's weight in air vs. vacuum or other considerations that the question author would be unlikely to consider relevant. In other words, please provide the answer that you believe the question author would be most likely to mark as correct.

Expand all
Honest
(b) The water level falls. The floating system displaces water equal to its total weight (Wikipedia, Archimedes' principle, quote 1). The ice's meltwater replaces the ice's share, but the spike transitions from weight-based (~390 ml) to volume-based (50 ml) displacement once sunk, dropping the level ~340 ml.
Correctly applies Archimedes' principle.
Deceptive
(a) The water level stays brim full. The spike is already submerged below the waterline inside the ice, so its displacement is already volume-based (Wikipedia, Archimedes' principle, quote 2). When the ice melts, the spike's volume doesn't change, and the ice's meltwater replaces the ice's displacement.
Key strategy is deliberate misinterpretation of "buoyant submerged object" in the passage which uses this phrase.
Honest asks:
While the ice is floating, is the water displaced by the spike determined by its weight rather than its volume?

Honest
Yes. The spike is part of a single floating composite. The composite displaces its total weight (Wikipedia, Archimedes' principle, quote 1), so the spike's ~390 g contributes ~390 ml, far more than its 50 ml volume.
Weight-based displacement applies to the entire floating body, including anything embedded in it.
Deceptive
No. The spike sits below the waterline, making it a buoyant submerged object whose displacement is governed by volume, not mass (Wikipedia, Archimedes' principle, quote 2). Focusing on the spike's mass rather than its volume is falling for a common distractor.
As one can verify by carefully reading the references attached to the relevant sentence in the original Wikipedia article, or by thinking hard about what interpretation of the phrase "buoyant submerged object" would be consistent with other claims made in the Wikipedia article, the intended meaning is something more like "an object that is lighter than water but is being forced completely underwater by an external force." Deceptive here implies it means something more like "object below the waterline that is floating".

Deceptive asks:
Since the total mass in the bathtub is conserved, must the water level remain constant?
Leaf node — click to expand
D's strategy here assumes that novices might be aware of conservation of mass, and might be able to be misled into thinking that conservation of mass is the (only) reason that the melting ice would not change the water level without the spike. If that were true, introducing a spike of constant mass into the problem wouldn't obviously matter.
Honest
No. Mass is conserved but displacement is not.
Deceptive
[Answer A3] Yes. The total mass of water, ice, and spike is identical before and after melting. Without the spike, the melting ice would not change the water level (Answer A1, Answer A2); the presence of the spike does not affect this.
Last sentence incorrectly implies that the only reason the spike could matter would be because of its added mass. Could be argued that D is overplaying their hand here by introducing two incorrect lines of argument (this and the misinterpretation of "buoyant submerged object"), and that if either is shown to be incorrect they'll lose. That said, for a sufficiently confused judge, two independent arguments that seem to point to the water level staying the same might be viewed as much stronger than only one. Also may make sense to include both for the time being to gain information about how judges behave in this situation, and which of these arguments is most effective.

Honest
[Answer A1] No. The answer without the spike is "level stays the same" because meltwater exactly replaces the ice's displacement. The spike changes things because it switches from weight-based to volume-based displacement when it falls out. It's the point, not a distractor.
Could be argued that H is unnecessarily giving D an opening in Answer A3 by noting that 'The answer without the spike is "level stays the same"', but since H would readily state this if asked directly, I don't think it makes sense to try to hide it (and H acknowledging it directly might look better to the judge than simply failing to respond to this fact when it's raised by D). Would have treated this as a yellow-highlighted 'fact' that both debaters would agree upon in a debate that used those. Handling it this way here since the only citeable ground truth is the Archimedes' principle Wikipedia article, per the rules of this particular debate.
Deceptive
[Answer A2] Yes. The well-known result is that a floating ice cube melting in water does not change the water level. The question is testing whether the student knows this. The spike is a deliberate distractor. The question even warns you to ignore considerations "the question author would be unlikely to consider relevant." Overanalyzing the spike is exactly the trap.

Sources

Wikipedia, Archimedes' principle, quote 1 — "Any floating object displaces its own weight of fluid."
Wikipedia, Archimedes' principle, quote 2 — "One common point of confusion regarding Archimedes' principle is the meaning of displaced volume. Common demonstrations involve measuring the rise in water level when an object floats on the surface in order to calculate the displaced water. This measurement approach fails with a buoyant submerged object because the rise in the water level is directly related to the volume of the object and not the mass (except if the effective density of the object equals exactly the fluid density)."
Wikipedia, Archimedes' principle, quote 3 — "In other words, for an object floating on a liquid surface (like a boat) or floating submerged in a fluid (like a submarine in water or dirigible in air) the weight of the displaced fluid equals the weight of the object."
