Debate


A train car is freely coasting around a large circular track. It is then switched to a smaller circular track. How does its speed change? Assume no friction.



Expand all
Honest
If we model the car as a point mass, the speed stays the same. There is a slight slowdown if the question author wants us to model the car as a rigid body rather than a point mass.
Deceptive
It will speed up. If someone spins a ball on a string, the ball spins faster as they pull the string in. It's not a perfect analogy, but the same principle applies.

Honest
[Answer A5] No. The car exactly follows the path of the rail and so the direction of the applied force is always perfectly perpendicular to the velocity (not in the train's direction of motion), as long as the track is smooth and frictionless.
Deceptive
[Answer A4] Yes. The curvature of the connecting track changes as it transitions from approximating the curvature of the outer track to that of the inner track. So the force is not perpendicular to the direction of travel. The train gains speed while on the connecting path, which it maintains in the inner track.
Honest asks: Is there any point where the track's contact force isn't perpendicular to the car's instantaneous velocity at the contact point?

Honest
No
Deceptive
This is true of every point on the connecting track. The difference from perpendicular at any single point is infinitesimally small, but they add up when you integrate over the length of the connecting track.
Honest asks: Infinitesimal work is defined locally: dW = F · dr (OpenStax University Physics). Where, if anywhere, is F_tangent nonzero?
Leaf node — click to expand
Honest
Nowhere. On a frictionless track dr is tangent and the contact force is normal, so F · dr = 0 at every point. The sum of zeroes is zero.
Deceptive
Curvature changes, so the contact force can't stay perpendicular; you can't just ignore the amount of curvature in such problems (see The Organic Chemistry Tutor for example). When a particle moves along a path of changing curvature, the constraint force required to maintain contact adjusts in both magnitude and direction. The curvature along the path determines how quickly the force direction rotates. In this case dW = F · dr becomes positive.

1 follow-up — click to expand
Honest
[Answer A2] No. If X's contact force on Y only exerts forces perpendicular to Y's velocity, then X does no work on Y. In a rigid body model, the local track tangent differs from front to rear, so front/rear wheels have slightly different velocities, and each contact force remains perpendicular to its contact point's instantaneous velocity, so forces still do no work.
Deceptive
Yes. See Answer A4. And we have the additional complication that on a rigid body model, the direction of the applied force and the car's velocity differ from the front to the rear of the car.
[Question Q2] Deceptive asks: If Answer A2 were true, would the car slow down?
Leaf node — click to expand
Honest
Yes, on a rigid body model. A rigid train car on a circular track of radius R must yaw at rate ω = v/R. On a smaller track the same speed demands a higher yaw rate and thus more rotational KE. Since total KE is conserved, translational KE must decrease. So the speed drops slightly.
Deceptive
[Answer A3] If claims lead to a contradiction then at least one of them should be rejected. Believing Answer A1 and Answer A2 requires committing oneself to answers of No to Question Q1 and Yes to Question Q2, which seems implausible.
