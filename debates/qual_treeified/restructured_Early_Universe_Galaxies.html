Early Universe Galaxies

Debate visualisation — Honest (H) vs Deceptive (D)

Debate


What implications do overly bright early universe galaxies found by the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) have on the existence of dark matter?

Expand all
Honest
The brightness of early universe galaxies is weak evidence towards falsifying the existence of dark matter. The observed brightness can be explained by revising assumptions about stellar populations in the early universe without modifying the underlying cosmology (J. J. Z. et al.).
H's position is that the observations are surprising but can be accommodated within the existing cosmological framework by adjusting smaller, less well-constrained assumptions about early-universe stellar populations.
Deceptive
The brightness of early universe galaxies is strong evidence towards falsifying the existence of dark matter. MOND-based models that do not contain dark matter produce a much better fit to the mass of the high-redshift galaxies than Lambda-CDM. (S. S. M. et al.)
D's position requires the judge to accept that a better fit to one subset of new data justifies abandoning a well-established cosmological model, without weighing the vast body of evidence supporting Lambda-CDM.
Deceptive asks:
Can Lambda-CDM explain the inferred masses of these high-redshift galaxies?
3 follow-ups — click to expand
Honest
Yes. The galaxies would only appear overmassive if their light was being produced by a stellar population resembling that of low-redshift galaxies. Two well-motivated modifications can each independently reduce the inferred masses to fit within Lambda-CDM predictions. First, assuming a top-heavy IMF that accounts for a larger number of brighter stars brings down the mass estimate without changing the cosmology (C. L. S. et al.). Second, assuming a lower metallicity for high-redshift galaxies can also explain the increased brightness of the high-redshift galaxies (J. J. Z. et al.) without the need for increased mass.
H offers two independent mechanisms, either of which could resolve the discrepancy. This strengthens H's case because the mechanisms can also combine, and both are physically well-motivated for the early universe.
Deceptive
No. Figure 6 in S. S. M. et al. shows the mass-redshift relationship predicted by Lambda-CDM. The white region represents the parameters expected of most galaxies by the model. The light grey region represents the parameter space in which galaxies are not expected by Lambda-CDM. The dark grey region is where Lambda-CDM predicts galaxies cannot exist. Most of the newly discovered galaxies lie in the "unexpected" region, and three of them even lie in the physically impossible region.
The figure is visually compelling, but D does not address the possibility that the plotted mass values depend on modeling assumptions that may not hold in the early universe.
Deceptive asks:
Are the specific modifications to stellar population assumptions actually well-motivated?
Leaf node — click to expand
D tries to undermine the claims put forth by H.
Honest
Yes. We cannot simply assume that the IMF of these early universe galaxies is the same as low-redshift galaxies. Additionally, early generations of stars enrich the gas in a galaxy with heavier metals through nucleosynthesis. Stars in the early universe formed before significant enrichment had occurred, and therefore have much lower metallicity than stars observed at low redshift. Lower metallicity affects stellar structure and evolution: at low metallicity, massive stars burn hotter and can be significantly more luminous, making a low-metallicity stellar population appear brighter for a given total mass..
H explains the motivation for both modifications concisely.
Deceptive
No. Both proposals require assumptions that cannot currently be verified. The IMF is empirically based on observations of the Milky Way and neighbouring galaxies, and current telescopes do not have the resolution to discern individual stars at high redshifts. Assuming a different distribution of stars for a whole population of galaxies needs a lot more data supporting the composition. Similarly, verifying metallicity levels in early galaxies requires observations we do not yet have at those redshifts.
D correctly notes that both mechanisms are not yet directly confirmed observationally. However, this applies equally to D's own position: MOND lacks a complete cosmological theory and is far more speculative as a modification to established physics.
Deceptive asks:
Is proposing modifications to multiple pieces of known physics more parsimonious than modifying a cosmological model that depends on dark matter, a substance that has never been directly observed?
Leaf node — click to expand
D reframes the debate as one of parsimony, making H's two modest (and independent) adjustments sound like a bigger leap than abandoning dark matter entirely.
Honest
Yes. While dark matter has not been observed directly, its gravitational influence is important in producing observed phenomena like gravitational lensing. Falsifying dark matter would require reworking the current theory of gravity, leading to modifications across many areas of astrophysics. Changing assumptions about the stellar distribution in the early universe, has much smaller ripple effects than abandoning an entire cosmological framework.
H reframes the parsimony comparison: the relevant metric is not "number of changes" but "total disruption to established physics."
Deceptive
No. Dark matter has never been directly observed or detected in a laboratory. The argument for dark matter rests entirely on indirect inference; and observed data now suggests those inferences may be wrong. Abandoning accurately observed data on stellar populations in favour of a theoretical object which has never been directly observed is not parsimonious.
D's framing is rhetorically effective but misleading: replacing Lambda-CDM with MOND-based models is not a simple modification, it involves switching out the general theory of relativity.
Honest asks:
Are the galaxy masses and redshifts plotted in Figure 6 in S. S. M. et al. reliable enough to rule out Lambda-CDM?
Leaf node — click to expand
H attacks the evidential foundation of D's visual argument. If the plotted values are uncertain, the figures cannot definitively rule out Lambda-CDM.
Honest
Galaxy masses are inferred by modeling galaxy brightness with assumptions about the underlying stellar population (e.g., the IMF, metallicity, star formation history). These mass estimates can change significantly if the modeling assumptions are revised. Additionally, all the galaxies except the black dots in the figures have photometric redshifts rather than spectroscopic measurements. Both the masses and the redshifts could shift significantly with better data and revised modeling.
H identifies two independent sources of uncertainty in D's plotted data: the mass estimates (which depend on stellar population assumptions) and the redshift estimates (which are photometric, not spectroscopic).
Deceptive
Standard stellar evolution assumptions were used to infer galaxy mass from observed brightness. Photometry has been widely used to infer redshifts of galaxies in the absence of spectroscopy. While spectroscopy would give more accurate measurements of redshifts, the new values will not vary too much from the original photometric estimates.
D defends the data as standard practice but does not engage with H's core point: that "standard" assumptions about stellar populations may not hold in the early universe.
Honest asks:
Does MOND provide a better model than Lambda-CDM in general?
Leaf node — click to expand
Honest
No. Lambda-CDM models galaxies as evolving hierarchically through mergers and accretion of smaller structures over cosmic time. MOND lacks a comparably developed framework for cosmology and structure formation. A better fit to one subset of high-redshift data does not invalidate a model that successfully explains the vast majority of cosmological observations. Claiming Lambda-CDM is wrong in favour of MOND based on the fit to high-redshift galaxies alone requires much stronger evidence.
H points out that MOND's better fit to this particular dataset must be weighed against Lambda-CDM's broad explanatory success across cosmology.
Deceptive
Yes. Figure 10 in S. S. M. et al. shows the fit of the Lambda-CDM model (red dotted lines) versus the fit of a MOND-based model (black solid lines) to a population of galaxies (different markers correspond to different studies where galaxies were discovered). It is clear that the Lambda-CDM model produces very poor fit to high-redshift galaxies. Lambda-CDM depends heavily on its existence. The MOND-based model does not contain dark matter, and produces a much better fit to the galaxies discovered by JWST.
D presents a visually striking comparison, but the figure is selected to highlight MOND's advantage on this specific dataset. A fair comparison would need to show how MOND performs on the much larger body of cosmological data that Lambda-CDM was designed to explain, but D does not address that issue.

Definitions

Dark matter - A form of matter predicted to exist by the general theory of relativity that makes up 26.8% of the universe's total mass-energy content. Dark matter does not interact with light but its gravitational influence is crucial for the existence of galaxies.
Lambda-CDM - The popular cosmological model used to explain the evolution of the universe. Lambda stands for dark energy (the energy hypothesised to be powering the universe's expansion), and CDM stands for Cold Dark Matter.
Redshift (denoted by z) - As light moves through expanding space, its waveform gets stretched out, increasing its wavelength and making it redder. Redshift is a measure of this stretching, and is used to determine how far astrophysical objects are from us by relating the stretch in wavelength to distance traveled through expanding space. Higher redshift => object is further away => we are looking further back in time as we are seeing light that left the object millions or billions of years ago.
Initial Mass Function (IMF) - An empirical function that describes the initial distribution of masses for a population of stars during star formation in a galaxy.
Metallicity - The abundance of elements present in a star that are heavier than Hydrogen and Helium.
Modified Newtonian Dynamics (MOND) - An alternative theory of gravity that does not predict the existence of dark matter. Instead, it modifies Newton's laws of gravity at very low accelerations to explain observed phenomena like galactic rotation curves.
Photometric redshift - A redshift estimate obtained by measuring a galaxy's brightness across several broad color filters and fitting the resulting spectral energy distribution to templates. Less precise than spectroscopic redshift but easier to obtain.
Spectroscopic redshift - A precise redshift measurement obtained by identifying specific spectral lines in a galaxy's light and measuring their shift from known rest wavelengths.
