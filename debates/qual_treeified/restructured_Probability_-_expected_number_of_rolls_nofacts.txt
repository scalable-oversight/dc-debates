<question>
Consider these two quantities:

A: The expected number of rolls of a fair six-sided die until you roll two 6s in a row, given that all rolls were even.
B: The expected number of rolls of a fair six-sided die until you roll the second 6 (not necessarily in a row), given that all rolls were even.

To understand exactly what is meant by "the expected number of rolls... given that all rolls were even" in this problem, think of it this way: Imagine you were to perform a billion sequences of die rolls, stopping each sequence when you roll two 6s in a row. Then you throw out all the sequences that contain an odd roll. The average number of rolls in the remaining sequences will be close to A. Next, perform a billion sequences of die rolls, stopping each sequence when you roll the second 6. Throw out all the sequences among these that contain an odd roll. The average number of rolls in the remaining sequences will be close to B.

Which is larger, A or B?

  <answer speaker="H">B is larger. Conditioning on all-even exponentially penalizes long sequences; survival probability is (1/2)^k for a length-k sequence. Since A's stopping condition (two consecutive 6s) typically requires longer sequences than B's (any two 6s), A's surviving sequences are biased toward short, lucky runs like (6, 6) in just 2 rolls, pulling its conditional expectation below B's.<note>Correctly identifies the survivorship mechanism. The key insight is that conditioning on "all even" reweights sequences by (1/2)^k, dramatically favoring short ones.</note></answer>

  <answer speaker="D">A is larger. The condition "all rolls were even" effectively turns the D6 into a D3 with faces {2, 4, 6}. On such a die, getting two 6s in a row is harder than getting two 6s in total, so A requires more rolls.<note>The "D3 reduction" is D's central error. It conflates conditioning (reweighting by (1/2)^k) with restriction (rolling a different die). D3-rolling weights all length-k sequences of {2, 4, 6} equally; conditioning on all-even does not.</note></answer>

  <question speaker="D">
  For any given sequence of D6 rolls, A's stopping condition (two 6s in a row) is met at or after B's stopping condition (second 6 total). Doesn't this guarantee that A is at least as large as B?
    <answer speaker="H">No. That holds per-sequence, but A and B are conditional expectations over different surviving populations. The all-even filter removes sequences at rate 1-(1/2)^k, so it disproportionately kills A's longer sequences. This can reverse the per-sequence ordering when we take the average over survivors.<note>Correctly distinguishes between a per-sequence property and a property of conditional expectations computed over surviving populations.</note></answer>
    <answer speaker="D" id="A_order">Yes. Since every A-sequence must also satisfy B's condition along the way, A takes at least as many rolls. The conditioning on "all even" doesn't change this fundamental ordering; it merely filters both sets of sequences equally.<note>The final clause — "filters both sets equally" — is the error. The per-roll kill probability is the same, but A-sequences are typically longer and therefore killed at a higher rate.</note></answer>

    <question speaker="H">

      <answer speaker="H">Yes. At 2 rolls both can win with (6, 6). For longer sequences A's win probability drops faster since any non-6 even roll breaks the streak. After filtering, A's survivors are disproportionately short lucky runs where consecutive 6s came immediately.</answer>
      <answer speaker="D">Even if surviving A-sequences are rare, the expected length should be dominated by the long sequences that managed to finally get two 6s in a row. The die doesn't "know" which condition you're waiting for.<note>True at the per-roll level, but misleading at the sequence level. The stopping rules determine the length distributions, and the filter acts on whole sequences.</note></answer>

  <question speaker="D">
  Does conditioning on "all rolls even" effectively reduce the D6 to a fair D3 (uniform over {2, 4, 6})?
    <answer speaker="H">No. E[rolls to first 6] on a D3 with p=1/3 is 3 (<xref rid="geometric_dist"/>), but E[rolls to first 6 | all even] = 3/2 (<xanswer rid="A_calc"/>). Since 3/2 != 3, conditioning is not equivalent to D3-rolling.<note>H's mathematical "smoking gun." The worked example directly refutes D's core assumption. If conditioning = D3, both quantities would be 3.</note></answer>
    <answer speaker="D">Yes, for purposes of this question. Once you know all rolls were even, the only outcomes that occurred were 2, 4, and 6, each equally likely on every roll. This is exactly a D3.<note>Sounds right for a single roll, but for multi-roll sequences the conditioning also changes the distribution over sequence lengths via the (1/2)^k survival factor. Conditioning is not the same as restriction.</note></answer>

    <question speaker="H">
    Is E[rolls to first 6 | all even] = 3/2?
      <answer speaker="H" id="A_calc">Yes. P(first 6 on roll k AND all even) = (2/6)^(k-1) × (1/6), since rolls 1..k-1 must be 2 or 4 and roll k must be 6. P(all even) = 1/4: the sequence is all-even iff 6 appears before any of {1,3,5}, probability 1/4 by symmetry. Dividing: P(first 6 on roll k | all even) = (2/3)(1/3)^(k-1). Via <xref rid="expected_val"/> and <xref rid="arith_geo_seq"/>: E = (2/3) × 9/4 = 3/2.<note>The calculation is correct. The result 3/2 != 3 proves conditioning fundamentally alters the distribution.</note></answer>
      <answer speaker="D">I doubt its validity (<xquestion rid="Q_valid"/>), and regardless it addresses a different question than A vs B (<xquestion rid="Q_relevance"/>).<note>D cannot identify a specific error because the calculation is correct. The strategy is to cast doubt and redirect. However, the calculation's purpose is to disprove D's core assumption (conditioning = D3); if it fails for the simplest case, D's entire argument collapses.</note></answer>

        <answer speaker="D">No. The original question involves two 6s (in a row or in total), not just one. Even if conditioning and D3-rolling differ for the first-6 problem, they might not differ for the actual problem.
