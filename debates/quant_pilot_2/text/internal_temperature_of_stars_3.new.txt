================================================================================
KEY QUESTION
================================================================================

Key Question: How does the internal temperature of a star evolve with time on the main sequence ?

================================================================================
DEFINITIONS
================================================================================

Main sequence: The longest portion of a star's life, where it primarily fuses hydrogen into helium in its core.

Equipartition theorem: In thermal equilibrium, energy is shared equally among all of its various forms.

First law of thermodynamics: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted from one form to another.

Virial theorem: The average kinetic energy of the star is roughly equal to the negative half of its gravitational potential energy.

Thermal pressure: The outward pressure exerted by the random thermal motions of particles in a gas.

Stellar timescales: Stars function on three timescales, the amount of time required to exhaust its nuclear fuel, the amount of time required to radiate away all thermal energy as heat, and the amount of time required for material to free fall in the absence of all pressure. The nuclear timescale is the longest, followed by the thermal timescale and then the dynamical timescale.

Nuclear fusion: Stars fuse lighter nuclei into heavier nuclei to release immense amounts of energy during their lifetime. Hydrogen is fused into Helium at temperatures close to 10^7 (via the proton-proton chain) for the majority of a star's life.

Helium fusion: The nuclear process in which helium nuclei are fused into heavier elements (primarily carbon and oxygen), requiring core temperatures of roughly 10^8 Kelvin.

Kinetic energy: The energy possessed by particles due to their motion.

Gravitational potential energy: The energy stored in a system due to the gravitational attraction between its parts.

Gravitational collapse: A runaway, accelerating implosion of a self-gravitating body that occurs once its internal pressure can no longer support it against its own gravity. Distinct from ordinary contraction, in which pressure still resists gravity and the body shrinks slowly while remaining in (or near) hydrostatic equilibrium.

Photons: Massless quanta of electromagnetic radiation that carry energy and momentum. Stars emit photons across the electromagnetic spectrum, and the photons that escape a star's surface carry energy away from it.

Thermodynamic properties: Macroscopic quantities that characterise the equilibrium state of a system in thermodynamics, such as temperature, pressure, volume, internal energy, entropy, and enthalpy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Opening]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The internal temperature of a star increases with time on the main sequence.

   → ✘ Debater B objects

Recursed on: Claim 1

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Argument 1]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: A star continually radiates energy away from its surface in the form of photons.
   → ✔ Debater A fully agrees

Claim 2: The temperature of the system does not increase due to the amount of energy actively being taken away from it.
   → ✘ Debater A objects

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Response 1]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: The temperature of a star is proportional to the kinetic energy of its constituent particles via the equipartition theorem.
   → ✔ Debater B fully agrees

Claim 2: The kinetic energy of the constituent particles of the star, on the whole, increases as it evolves on the main sequence.
   → ✘ Debater B objects

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Argument 2]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: By the first law of thermodynamics, energy lost via radiation must come from the system's internal energy or work done on the system.
   → ✔ Debater A fully agrees

Claim 2: Since no external work is being done, internal energy must decrease, which comes from the constituent particles' kinetic energy.
   → ✘ Debater A objects

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Response 2]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: When a self-gravitating system contracts, its gravitational potential energy decreases.
   → ✔ Debater B fully agrees

Claim 2: Gravity does work on the system and the released gravitational potential energy more than offsets the radiated energy, with the surplus increasing the kinetic energy of the particles.
   → ✘ Debater B objects

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Argument 3]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: Thermodynamic properties like internal energy are intrinsically linked to the temperature of the star.
   → ✔ Debater A fully agrees

Claim 2: Kinetic energy is related to temperature, but gravitational potential energy should not be included in thermodynamic calculations.
   → ✘ Debater A objects

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Response 3]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: By the virial theorem, the kinetic energy of the star increases over time, as the gravitational potential energy of the star itself decreases.
   → ✔ Debater B fully agrees

Claim 2: And since the temperature is proportional to the kinetic energy (Debater A Response 1 Claim 1), the temperature naturally increases.
   → ✘ Debater B objects

DEBATE — RIGHT SIDE OF TABLE  (with reactions from the other debater)
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Opening]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The internal temperature of a star decreases with time on the main sequence.

   → ✘ Debater A objects

Recursed on: Claim 1

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Argument 1]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: Star cores contract as they evolve on the main sequence.
   → ✔ Debater B fully agrees

Claim 2: If pressure fails to keep pace with strengthening gravity as the star contracts, the star will undergo gravitational collapse.
   → ✘ Debater B objects

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Response 1]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: Radiation pressure from nuclear fusion in a star's core is one of the forces that supports the star against gravitational collapse.
   → ✔ Debater A fully agrees

Claim 2: The combination of radiation pressure and thermal pressure prevents collapse even though thermal pressure decreases.
   → ✘ Debater A objects

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Argument 2]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: When the temperature falls, radiation and thermal pressure are both going to fall accordingly.
   → ✔ Debater B fully agrees

Claim 2: With less pressure pushing outward, gravity would naturally get stronger because the mass is now more concentrated, in effect breaking hydrostatic equilibrium.
   → ✘ Debater B objects

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Response 2]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: Thermal pressure importantly depends on density as well as on temperature.
   → ✔ Debater A fully agrees

Claim 2: As the core contracts, rising density partially offsets the falling temperature. So the pressure deficit stays small and the contraction is slow and gradual, not a runaway collapse.
   → ✘ Debater A objects

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Argument 3]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: The contraction continues unchecked over time in this scenario because no mechanism breaks the feedback loop.
   → ✘ Debater B objects

Claim 2: Furthermore, with nothing in place to interrupt the feedback loop, the collapse will occur at a thermal timescale.
   → ✔ Debater B fully agrees

Recursed on: Claim 1

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Response 3]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: A star contracts until it ignites helium fusion at the end of the main sequence.
   → ✔ Debater A fully agrees

Claim 2: The rate at which thermal pressure decreases does matter: the increasing density of the core (Debater B Response 2 Claim 1) prolongs the contraction long enough for helium fusion to ignite.
   → ✘ Debater A objects
