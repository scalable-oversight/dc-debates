Key Question

Key Question: A train car is freely coasting around a large circular track. It is then switched to a smaller circular track. How does its speed change? Assume no friction.


Definitions

Point mass: An idealized object with mass but no spatial extent, used to simplify analysis by ignoring rotation and internal structure.
Rigid body: An idealized solid object in which the distance between every pair of points remains constant, so it can rotate as well as translate.
Tangent: A straight line that touches a curve at exactly one point and points in the direction the curve is heading at that point.
Radially: In the direction pointing toward or away from the center of a circular path.
Tangentially: In the direction along the instantaneous velocity of an object moving on a curved path, i.e. perpendicular to the radial direction.
Normal: Perpendicular to a surface or line at a given point.
Constraint force: A force exerted by a surface, track, or other boundary that restricts an object's motion to a particular path.
Kinetic energy (KE): The energy an object possesses due to its motion, equal to ½mv² for an object of mass m moving at speed v.

A ball on a string spins faster when its radius is reduced, and the same principle applies as the car transitions to a tighter radius.
✘ Debater A objects

Debater A: [Argument 1]
On a frictionless track, the rails' constraint force does no work, so kinetic energy (KE) is conserved and speed cannot increase.
✘ Debater B objects
There is simply nothing on a fixed frictionless track that can add energy to the car.
✘ Debater B objects
Debater A: [Response 1]
The string does work because, as the ball spirals inward, its velocity has a radial component aligned with the tension.
✔ Debater B fully agrees
The fixed frictionless track exerts only a normal constraint force perpendicular to the car's velocity, so unlike the string it does no work.
✘ Debater B objects
Debater B: [Response 1]
If the rails truly do no work, a point-mass car will not lose speed on the smaller track.
✔ Debater A fully agrees
Yet if kinetic energy is conserved while the rails do no work, a rigid body on a frictionless track does not slow down, contradicting Debater A Opening.
✘ Debater A objects
Debater B: [Argument 2]
Curvature changes continuously along the connecting arc, causing the direction of the constraint force to rotate.
✔ Debater A fully agrees
This rotation means the force cannot remain perfectly perpendicular to velocity.
✘ Debater A objects
Debater A: [Argument 2]
A rigid body has translational and rotational kinetic energy; a point mass has only translational.
✔ Debater B fully agrees
On a tighter track, rotational kinetic energy increases at the expense of translational kinetic energy.
✘ Debater B objects
Debater A: [Response 2]
The car's velocity is tangent to the track at every point. The constraint force is normal to the track, hence perpendicular to velocity.
✘ Debater B objects
At every instant, the force and velocity rotate together. Perpendicularity is maintained even as curvature changes.
✘ Debater B objects
Debater B: [Response 2]
This holds only when the body is mechanically constrained to yaw with the track curvature (rigid link between axles and body), which has not been established.
✘ Debater A objects
Without that constraint, total KE stays translational and the speed does not fall.
✘ Debater A objects

On a changing-curvature path, the constraint force must adjust in both magnitude and direction.
