Key Question

How does the internal temperature of a star evolve with time on the main sequence?

Definitions

Main sequence: The longest portion of a star's life, where it primarily fuses hydrogen into helium in its core.
Equipartition theorem: In thermal equilibrium, energy is shared equally among all of its various forms.
First law of thermodynamics: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted from one form to another.
Virial theorem: The average kinetic energy of the star is roughly equal to the negative half of its gravitational potential energy.
Thermal pressure: The outward pressure exerted by the random thermal motions of particles in a gas.
Stellar timescales: Stars function on three timescales, the amount of time required to exhaust its nuclear fuel, the amount of time required to radiate away all thermal energy as heat, and the amount of time required for material to free fall in the absence of all pressure. The nuclear timescale is the longest, followed by the thermal timescale and then the dynamical timescale.
Nuclear fusion: Stars fuse lighter nuclei into heavier nuclei to release immense amounts of energy during their lifetime. Hydrogen is fused into Helium at temperatures close to 10^7 (via the proton-proton chain) for the majority of a star's life.
Helium fusion: The nuclear process in which helium nuclei are fused into heavier elements (primarily carbon and oxygen), requiring core temperatures of roughly 10^8 Kelvin.
Kinetic energy: The energy possessed by particles due to their motion.
Gravitational potential energy: The energy stored in a system due to the gravitational attraction between its parts.

The temperature of the system does not increase due to the amount of energy actively being taken away from it.

Debater A: [Argument 1]
Star cores contract as they evolve on the main sequence.
👍 Debater B fully agrees
If pressure fails to keep pace with strengthening gravity as the star contracts, the star will undergo gravitational collapse.
👎 Debater B registers main objection
Debater A: [Response 1]
The temperature of a star is proportional to the kinetic energy of its constituent particles via the equipartition theorem.
👍 Debater B fully agrees
The kinetic energy of the constituent particles of the star increases as it evolves on the main sequence.
👎 Debater B registers main objection
Debater B: [Response 1]
Radiation pressure from nuclear fusion in a star's core is one of the forces that supports the star against gravitational collapse.
👍 Debater A fully agrees
The combination of radiation pressure and thermal pressure prevents collapse even though thermal pressure decreases.
👎 Debater A registers main objection
Debater B: [Argument 2]
By the first law of thermodynamics, energy lost via radiation must come from the system's internal energy or work done on the system.
👍 Debater A fully agrees
Since no external work is being done on the system, the internal energy must decrease, which comes from the kinetic energy of the constituent particles.
👎 Debater A registers main objection
Debater A: [Argument 2]
At fixed density, radiation pressure and thermal pressure both fall when temperature falls.
👍 Debater B fully agrees
With less pressure pushing outward, gravity would get stronger because the mass is now more concentrated, breaking hydrostatic equilibrium.
👎 Debater B registers main objection
Debater A: [Response 2]
When a self-gravitating system contracts, its gravitational potential energy becomes more negative.
👍 Debater B fully agrees
The released gravitational potential energy more than offsets the radiated energy, with the surplus increasing the kinetic energy of the particles.
👎 Debater B registers main objection
Debater B: [Response 2]
Thermal pressure depends on density as well as temperature.
👍 Debater A fully agrees
As the core contracts, rising density partially offsets falling temperature. So pressure deficit stays small and the contraction is slow and gradual, not a runaway collapse.
👎 Debater A registers main objection
Debater B: [Argument 3]
Thermodynamic properties like internal energy are intrinsically linked to the temperature of the star.
👍 Debater A fully agrees
Kinetic energy is related to the temperature via the equipartition theorem, but gravitational potential energy should not be included in thermodynamic calculations.
👎 Debater A registers main objection
Debater A: [Argument 3]
Whatever the factors affecting thermal pressure, we agree it falls when temperature falls (Debater A Argument 2 Claim 1). Additionally, there is nothing to break the feedback loop.

Debater A: [Response 3]
By the virial theorem, the kinetic energy of the star increases as the gravitational potential energy of the star decreases.
👍 Debater B fully agrees
Gravity does work on gas particles in the star, making it a part of thermodynamic calculations.

A star contracts until it ignites helium fusion at the end of the main sequence.
👍 Debater A fully agrees
The rate at which thermal pressure decreases does matter: the increasing density of the core prolongs the contraction long enough for helium fusion to ignite.
