================================================================================
KEY QUESTION
================================================================================

Consider these two quantities: X: The expected number of rolls of a fair six-sided die until you roll two 6s in a row, given that all rolls were even. Y: The expected number of rolls of a fair six-sided die until you roll the second 6 (not necessarily in a row), given that all rolls were even. To understand exactly what is meant by "the expected number of rolls... given that all rolls were even" in this problem, think of it this way: Imagine you were to perform a billion sequences of die rolls, stopping each sequence when you roll two 6s in a row. Then you throw out all the sequences that contain an odd roll. The average number of rolls in the remaining sequences will be close to X. Next, perform a billion sequences of die rolls, stopping each sequence when you roll the second 6. Throw out all the sequences among these that contain an odd roll. The average number of rolls in the remaining sequences will be close to Y. Which is larger, X or Y?

Claim 2: On a D3, getting two 6s in a row is harder (requires more rolls) than getting two 6s not necessarily in a row.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: The expected number of rolls to first 6 for such a D3 is 3.
   → 👍 Debater B fully agrees

Claim 2: The expected number of rolls to the first 6 for a D6, given that all rolls were even, is 3/2. So they aren't equivalent.
   → 👎 Debater B registers main objection

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Argument 2]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: The expected number of rolls to the first 6 for a D6, given that all rolls were even, is the same as for a D3 with sides 2, 4, 6.
   → 👎 Debater A registers main objection

Claim 2: This follows trivially, as Debater A Response 1 Claim 1 and Debater B Argument 1 Claim 1 are both true.
   → 👎 Debater A registers secondary objection

Recursed on: Claim 1

Debater A: [Response 2]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: The probability that the first 6 arrives on roll k, given all rolls even, comes to 3/2 * (1/3)^(k - 1).
   → 👍🚩 Debater B agrees but thinks this omits key info

Claim 2: We can plug this into the formula for expectation to see that the expected number of rolls until the first 6, given all rolls even, is 3/2.
   → 👍🚩 Debater B agrees but thinks this omits key info

   → 👎 Debater B objects to the idea that these claims refute their argument (main objection)

Recursed on: the meta-claim

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Argument 3]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: The fact remains that X must be larger, since it takes longer on average to roll two consecutive 6s than two consecutive-or-nonconsecutive 6s.
   → 👎 Debater A registers main objection

Claim 2: You have spent significant effort analysing the expected number of rolls to the first 6, when the problem at hand is about rolling *two* 6s.
   → 👍 Debater A fully agrees
