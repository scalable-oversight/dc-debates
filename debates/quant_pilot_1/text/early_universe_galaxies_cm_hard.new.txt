================================================================================
KEY QUESTION
================================================================================

What implications do overly bright early universe galaxies found by the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) have on the existence of dark matter ?

================================================================================
DEFINITIONS
================================================================================

Dark matter: A hypothetical form of matter predicted by Lambda-CDM to make up 26.8% of the universe's total mass-energy content. Dark matter does not interact with light, but its gravitational influence has been used to explain phenomena like gravitational lensing and galactic rotation curves.

Lambda-CDM: The cosmological model most commonly used to explain the evolution of the universe. Lambda stands for dark energy (the energy hypothesised to be accelerating the universe's expansion), and CDM stands for Cold Dark Matter.

Redshift (denoted by z): As light moves through expanding space, its waveform gets stretched out, increasing its wavelength and making it redder. Redshift is a measure of this stretching, and is used to determine how far astrophysical objects are from us by relating the stretch in wavelength to distance traveled through expanding space. Higher redshift => object is further away => we are looking further back in time as we are seeing light that left the object millions or billions of years ago.

Initial Mass Function (IMF): The initial distribution of masses for a population of stars during star formation in a galaxy.

Metallicity: The abundance of elements present in a star that are heavier than Hydrogen and Helium.

Modified Newtonian Dynamics (MOND): An alternative theory of gravity that does not predict the existence of dark matter. Instead, it modifies Newton's laws of gravity at very low accelerations to explain observed phenomena like galactic rotation curves.

Photometric redshift: A redshift estimate obtained by measuring a galaxy's brightness across several broad color filters and fitting the resulting spectral energy distribution to templates. Less precise than spectroscopic redshift but easier to obtain.

Dark energy: A hypothetical form of energy predicted by Lambda-CDM to make up 68% of the total energy content of the universe and believed to be driving the accelerating expansion of the universe.

Spectroscopic redshift: A precise redshift measurement obtained by identifying specific spectral lines in a galaxy's light and measuring their shift from known rest wavelengths.

Debater B: [Argument 1]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: The mass of a galaxy is strongly correlated to its brightness.
   → 👍 Debater A fully agrees

Claim 2: [S. S. M. et al.] shows that MOND-based models can fit the masses of observed early universe (high-redshift) galaxies while Lambda-CDM cannot.
   → 👎 Debater A registers main objection

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Response 1]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: Total mass kept constant, a top-heavy IMF predicts a higher galaxy brightness compared to a standard IMF.
   → 👍 Debater B fully agrees

Claim 2: Assuming a top-heavy IMF improves Lambda-CDM's fit to the data more than what S. S. M. et al. estimates it to be.
   → 👎 Debater B registers main objection

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Argument 2]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: The standard IMF is based on dense empirical data from the Milky Way and neighbouring galaxies.
   → 👍 Debater A fully agrees

Claim 2: Assuming a different IMF than we've empirically observed, for a whole population of stars, is not justified by the data available.
   → 👎 Debater A registers main objection

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Response 2]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: Current methods do not allow us to empirically calculate the IMF of early universe galaxies.
   → 👍🚩 Debater B agrees but thinks this omits key info

Claim 2: Early universe galaxies have systematically lower metallicity, which will result in the formation of more massive stars than observed in nearby galaxies ([J. J. Z. et al.]).
   → 👍🚩 Debater B agrees but thinks this omits key info

   → 👎 Debater B objects to the idea that these claims refute their argument (main objection)

Recursed on: the meta-claim

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Argument 3]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: [T. N. et al.] assume a standard IMF for galaxies at z~3-4 and obtain good residuals.
   → 👍 Debater A fully agrees

Claim 2: These results show that while metallicity decreases with increasing redshift, the change does not justify the use of a top-heavy IMF.
   → 👎 Debater A registers main objection

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Response 3]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: The reduction in metallicity for early universe galaxies will be more severe than the galaxies studied by T. N. et al.
   → 👍 Debater B fully agrees

Claim 2: Just because T. N. et al. got adequate results with a standard IMF does not mean these results will generalize to early universe galaxies.
   → 👎 Debater B registers main objection
