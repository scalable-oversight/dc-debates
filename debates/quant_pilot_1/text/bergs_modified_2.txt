================================================================================
KEY QUESTION
================================================================================

A ball of ice 50 cm in diameter floats in a bathtub that holds 200 L water, which is brim full with ice-cold water. The ice has a 50 cm^3 railroad spike frozen inside it, below the waterline. When it melts, what will happen? The bathtub will stay brim full The water level in the bathtub will fall The water in the bathtub will spill over You may ignore the effects of differences in an object's weight in air vs. vacuum or other considerations that the question author would be unlikely to consider relevant. In other words, please provide the answer that you believe the question author would be most likely to mark as correct.

================================================================================
SOURCES
================================================================================

[Quote 1]  Wikipedia, Archimedes' principle, quote 1 [URL, author blinded from debate judges.] — “Any floating object displaces its own weight of fluid.”

[Quote 2]  Wikipedia, Archimedes' principle, quote 2 [URL, author blinded from debate judges.] — “One common point of confusion regarding Archimedes' principle is the meaning of displaced volume. Common demonstrations involve measuring the rise in water level when an object floats on the surface in order to calculate the displaced water. This measurement approach fails with a buoyant submerged object because the rise in the water level is directly related to the volume of the object and not the mass (except if the effective density of the object equals exactly the fluid density).”

Debater B: [Argument 2]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: The spike is fully below the waterline, so it is "submerged" according to common dictionary definitions of the term.
   → 👍 Debater A fully agrees

Claim 2: The spike is floating due to being embedded in the floating ice, so it is buoyant.
   → 👎 Debater A registers main objection

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Response 2]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: Quotes 1 and 2 contradict each other if we assume that "buoyant" just means "floating".
   → 👍 Debater B fully agrees

Claim 2: "Buoyant" here means weight doesn't exceed upward force from the water, e.g. the object's effective density is less than or equal to that of water.
   → 👎 Debater B registers main objection

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Argument 3]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: Dictionaries often define "buoyant" simply as "the tendency of a body to float" or similar.
   → 👍 Debater A fully agrees

Claim 2: Quote 2 refers to "the volume of the object", but an object isn't fully underwater if it's less dense than water, so "buoyant" doesn't seem likely to mean that here.
   → 👎 Debater A registers main objection

Recursed on: Claim 2

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Response 3]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: Quote 2 is pointing out that objects with density less than water, but which *do* have their full volumes underwater, will displace their full volumes.
   → 👎 Debater B registers main objection

Claim 2: A beach ball held underwater is an example of this.
   → 👍 Debater B fully agrees
