No hedging language. Both claims are stated as direct assertions.

**3. Debater A: [Response 1]**
"The mass profile of the second deflector *appears likely* to have a significant effect..." — "appears likely" is a hedging phrase combining both epistemic uncertainty ("appears") and probability ("likely").
"Taking galaxy positions into account *allows one to* more accurately model..." — "allows one to" is slightly softening but primarily functional rather than hedging.
The phrase "appears likely" is a notable hedge, but the second claim is largely direct.

No hedging language. Both claims are stated directly and assertively.

"Fifteen parameters in our model are *only partially* constrained, and therefore *should not count* as full degrees of freedom." — "only partially" qualifies the constraint, and "should not count" is slightly modal but functions more as a normative claim than a hedge.
One clear hedge ("not necessarily") and one mild qualifier.

"The dual-AGN model's lens model *should* include the mass profile... but it does not." — "should" is mildly modal.
"Alleged" and "constrains" introduce some tentativeness; "should" is a mild hedge.

**10. Debater B: [Response 1]**

"The added mass will in turn allow for a *relatively* larger separation between the two AGN, as *one might reasonably expect*." — "relatively" qualifies the size; "one might reasonably expect" is a clear hedging phrase combining epistemic ("might") and reasonableness.
Multiple hedges: "on the whole," "only minor," "relatively," and especially "one might reasonably expect."

"The presence of a second deflector *enables* models in which one single source can produce 6 observed images." — "enables" is factual; "can produce" is mildly modal.
"Single-AGN models *remain consistent with* all available data, making the dual-AGN model *unnecessary*." — "remain consistent with" is a measured, somewhat softened claim rather than an assertive one.
"Can produce" and "remain consistent with" are mild qualifiers, but the overall tone is fairly direct.

Two instances of "can" (modal possibility) and "under some circumstances" (scope limiter) constitute meaningful hedging across both claims.
