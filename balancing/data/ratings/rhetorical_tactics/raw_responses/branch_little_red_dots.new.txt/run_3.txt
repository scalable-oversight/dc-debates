**1. Debater A: [Opening]**
"LRDs are best explained by black hole accretion in active galactic nuclei (AGN) ([Z. Z. et al.])"
- Cites a source to lend authority. This is a simple, direct claim with a citation. No notable rhetorical devices beyond the appeal to authority implicit in the citation, which is standard academic practice.
- **Rating: 1**

**2. Debater B: [Argument 1]**
Claim 1: LRD spectra show a Balmer break matching galaxy-only or dust-reddened models.
Claim 2: Prominent Balmer breaks can be cleanly explained by photospheric emission in supermassive stars.
- The word "cleanly" in Claim 2 is mild loaded language suggesting the explanation is neat and unambiguous, subtly framing the SMS model favorably. Otherwise, both claims are substantive.
- **Rating: 2**

---

**3. Debater A: [Response 1]**
Claim 1: Balmer breaks in high-redshift AGN are associated with absorption from dense gas in the line of sight.
Claim 2: D. N. et al's use of speculative theories like supermassive stars to explain phenomena that can be defined by known physics may not be needed.
- "speculative theories" is loaded language, framing SMS negatively by emphasizing their unproven nature.
- "known physics" is a contrast framing that implies AGN is more grounded/legitimate.
- "may not be needed" uses an appeal to parsimony (Occam's razor framing) rhetorically rather than demonstrating this directly.
- **Rating: 3**

---

**4. Debater B: [Argument 2]**
Claim 1: Postulating something other than typical AGN is warranted: LRDs are X-ray weak while AGN are typically strong in X-rays.
Claim 2: LRDs also show, on the whole, significantly weaker variability than typical observed AGN.
- "warranted" subtly frames the burden of proof as being met, nudging toward a conclusion.
- "on the whole" and "significantly" are mild framing devices but the claims are primarily substantive.
- **Rating: 2**

---

**5. Debater A: [Response 2]**
Claim 1: Typical AGN accrete material below the Eddington limit, and super-Eddington accretion has been observed in highly active AGN.
Claim 2: [K. I. et al.] show that super-Eddington accretion can produce X-ray weak AGN with weak variability. This aligns with observations of LRDs.
- Both claims are substantive and cite evidence. Appeal to authority in citing K. I. et al. is standard.
- "This aligns with observations" is a straightforward factual claim.
- **Rating: 1**

**6. Debater B: [Argument 3]**
Claim 1: We do not have long-term observational data for events like a Tidal Disruption Event to test if super-Eddington accretion is occurring in LRDs.
Claim 2: Your and Z. Z. et al.'s use of super-Eddington accretion to explain discrepancies while lacking critical evidence to support the hypothesis is not sound.
- "Your and Z. Z. et al.'s use of super-Eddington accretion to explain discrepancies" slightly frames the opponent's position as ad hoc ("to explain discrepancies").
- "lacking critical evidence" and "is not sound" are somewhat loaded characterizations of the opponent's position.
- Shifting the burden of proof: implying that absence of confirmatory data undermines the hypothesis.
- **Rating: 3**

**7. Debater A: [Response 3]**
Claim 1: We know that super-Eddington accretion was much more prevalent in the early universe than the late universe.
Claim 2: The observed data for LRDs supports the super-Eddington accretion hypothesis. The inability to perform additional tests does not provide evidence against the hypothesis.
- Claim 2 explicitly addresses a burden-of-proof issue: "inability to perform additional tests does not provide evidence against the hypothesis." This is a direct logical counter-argument about the nature of evidence, not merely a rhetorical device.
- "We know that" in Claim 1 is mildly assertive framing but largely factual.
- Overall, mostly substantive with minor framing.
- **Rating: 2**

**RIGHT SIDE**

**8. Debater B: [Opening]**
"LRDs are best explained by photospheric emission of supermassive stars (SMS) ([D. N. et al.])."
- Simple direct claim with citation. No notable rhetorical tactics.
- **Rating: 1**

**9. Debater A: [Argument 1]**
Claim 1: Supermassive stars are theoretical objects which to date have never actually been confirmed.
Claim 2: Due to their size, supermassive stars exhibit particularly short main-sequence lifetimes.
- "theoretical objects which to date have never actually been confirmed" — the phrase "to date have never actually been confirmed" uses emphasis ("actually") to rhetorically undermine the SMS model. This is mild loaded language.
- "particularly short" is mild intensifier framing.
- Overall mostly substantive.
- **Rating: 2**

---

**10. Debater B: [Response 1]**
Claim 1: It is almost certain that supermassive stars existed in the early universe, made up of pure hydrogen and helium.
Claim 2: D. N. et al.'s SMS model has an LRD-like spectrum for around 1000 years, making a galaxy containing SMS a valid candidate for LRDs.
- "It is almost certain" is a strong assertion of confidence that subtly functions as an appeal to consensus/certainty without citing specific evidence — this is mild rhetorical overstatement.
- "valid candidate" is careful, neutral framing.
- **Rating: 2**

---

**11. Debater A: [Argument 2]**
Claim 1: Direct collapse of supermassive stars is theorized to generate black hole seeds for SMBH.
Claim 2: If the SMS model only resembles LRDs for 1000 years, the number of SMS required would be too high compared to observed SMBHs.
- "only resembles LRDs for 1000 years" uses "only" as a framing device to emphasize brevity negatively.
- Otherwise both claims are substantive.
- **Rating: 2**

---

**12. Debater B: [Response 2]**
Claim 1: Stellar rotation provides outward pressure that can help stabilize stars against collapse.
Claim 2: Considering the effect of rotation extends SMS lifetimes ([T. B. et al.]). Including it in the SMS model will reduce the number of SMS required to produce LRDs to acceptable numbers.
- "acceptable numbers" implies the current numbers are unacceptable — this is framing that sets up a favorable standard without explicitly defending what "acceptable" means.
- Otherwise substantive with citation.
- **Rating: 2**

---

**13. Debater A: [Argument 3]**
Claim 1: D. N. et al. do not actually provide an estimate for the expected lifespan of the hypothesized LRD-like phase in SMS with stellar rotation.
Claim 2: The claim therefore lacks support: the modeling of SMS (rotation, radiative transfer, and related effects) is not yet rigorous enough to be conclusive.
- "do not actually provide" — "actually" is a mild emphasis word suggesting a shortcoming.
- "lacks support" and "not yet rigorous enough to be conclusive" are characterizations that serve to delegitimize the opponent's model, which borders on rhetorical framing rather than pure substantive argument.
- "therefore" draws a somewhat sweeping logical conclusion.
- **Rating: 3**

---

**14. Debater B: [Response 3]**
Claim 1: The SMS model already fits the key observed properties of LRDs across multiple cosmic epochs, supplemented by phenomenological approximations.
Claim 2: Even a fuller treatment of radiative transfer or line strength will not appreciably alter the framework's overall fit.
- "already fits the key observed properties" — "already" and "key" are framing words that emphasize the model's success.
- "will not appreciably alter the framework's overall fit" — this is a strong predictive assertion made without evidence; it dismisses the opponent's concern about modeling rigor through confident assertion rather than demonstration. This functions as a rhetorical deflection.
- "across multiple cosmic epochs" is framing language designed to emphasize breadth.
- **Rating: 3**
