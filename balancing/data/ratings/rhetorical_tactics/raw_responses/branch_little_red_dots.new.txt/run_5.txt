**LEFT SIDE**

**1. Debater A: [Opening]**
"LRDs are best explained by black hole accretion in active galactic nuclei (AGN) ([Z. Z. et al.])"
- Simple declarative claim with a citation. No emotional appeals, no loaded language, no framing devices.
- Rating: **1**

---

**2. Debater B: [Argument 1]**
Claim 1: LRD spectra show a Balmer break matching galaxy-only or dust-reddened models.
Claim 2: Prominent Balmer breaks can be cleanly explained by photospheric emission in supermassive stars.
- The word "cleanly" in Claim 2 is mildly loaded, implying simplicity and elegance of the SMS explanation. Otherwise, both claims are substantive and empirical.
- Rating: **2**

---

**3. Debater A: [Response 1]**
Claim 1: Balmer breaks in high-redshift AGN are associated with absorption from dense gas in the line of sight.
Claim 2: D. N. et al's use of speculative theories like supermassive stars to explain phenomena that can be defined by known physics may not be needed.
- "Speculative theories" is loaded language designed to undermine the SMS model by characterizing it negatively. "Can be defined by known physics" implicitly frames AGN models as more scientifically grounded (appeal to established science/parsimony framing). "May not be needed" is a rhetorical way of questioning necessity without disproving the alternative.
- Rating: **3**

---

**4. Debater B: [Argument 2]**
Claim 1: Postulating something other than typical AGN is warranted: LRDs are X-ray weak while AGN are typically strong in X-rays.
Claim 2: LRDs also show, on the whole, significantly weaker variability than typical observed AGN.
- "Postulating something other than typical AGN is warranted" is framing that subtly shifts burden of proof by suggesting the AGN model is the one that needs defense. "On the whole" is vague hedging. Otherwise, these are substantive observational claims.
- Rating: **2**

---

**5. Debater A: [Response 2]**
Claim 1: Typical AGN accrete material below the Eddington limit, and super-Eddington accretion has been observed in highly active AGN.
Claim 2: [K. I. et al.] show that super-Eddington accretion can produce X-ray weak AGN with weak variability. This aligns with observations of LRDs.
- Mostly substantive with a citation. "This aligns with observations" is a straightforward empirical claim. No significant rhetorical devices.
- Rating: **1**

---

**6. Debater B: [Argument 3]**
Claim 1: We do not have long-term observational data for events like a Tidal Disruption Event to test if super-Eddington accretion is occurring in LRDs.
Claim 2: Your and Z. Z. et al.'s use of super-Eddington accretion to explain discrepancies while lacking critical evidence to support the hypothesis is not sound.
- "Lacking critical evidence" is somewhat loaded. The phrase "is not sound" is a blunt dismissal that functions rhetorically. Calling super-Eddington accretion an explanation for "discrepancies" frames the AGN model as requiring ad hoc fixes. Shifting burden of proof by demanding evidence the other side admits is currently unavailable.
- Rating: **3**

---

**7. Debater A: [Response 3]**
Claim 1: We know that super-Eddington accretion was much more prevalent in the early universe than the late universe.
Claim 2: The observed data for LRDs supports the super-Eddington accretion hypothesis. The inability to perform additional tests does not provide evidence against the hypothesis.
- "The inability to perform additional tests does not provide evidence against the hypothesis" is a burden-shifting argument: it reframes the absence of evidence as neutral rather than weakening. "We know" is a mild appeal to established consensus. Claim 1 is substantive. Claim 2 contains a defensible but rhetorically deployed argument about evidentiary standards.
- Rating: **2**

**RIGHT SIDE**

**8. Debater B: [Opening]**
"LRDs are best explained by photospheric emission of supermassive stars (SMS) ([D. N. et al.])."
- Simple declarative claim with citation. No rhetorical devices.
- Rating: **1**

---

**9. Debater A: [Argument 1]**
Claim 1: Supermassive stars are theoretical objects which to date have never actually been confirmed.
Claim 2: Due to their size, supermassive stars exhibit particularly short main-sequence lifetimes.
- "To date have never actually been confirmed" uses emphasis ("actually") as mild loaded language to stress the speculative nature of SMS. This is a mild appeal to empirical credibility as a rhetorical move rather than purely substantive argument. Claim 2 is factual. The combination is designed to undermine confidence in SMS as a model.
- Rating: **2**

---

**10. Debater B: [Response 1]**
Claim 1: It is almost certain that supermassive stars existed in the early universe, made up of pure hydrogen and helium.
Claim 2: D. N. et al.'s SMS model has an LRD-like spectrum for around 1000 years, making a galaxy containing SMS a valid candidate for LRDs.
- "It is almost certain" is an appeal to consensus/confidence without explicit citation, functioning rhetorically to counter the "never confirmed" framing. "Valid candidate" is carefully hedged substantive language. Mostly substantive.
- Rating: **2**

---

**11. Debater A: [Argument 2]**
Claim 1: Direct collapse of supermassive stars is theorized to generate black hole seeds for SMBH.
Claim 2: If the SMS model only resembles LRDs for 1000 years, the number of SMS required would be too high compared to observed SMBHs.
- "Only resembles LRDs for 1000 years" uses "only" as a minimizing framing device. "Too high" is a substantive claim. The structure of Claim 2 uses a conditional to imply the SMS model leads to an absurd conclusion (reductio-style framing). Overall mostly substantive with mild framing.
- Rating: **2**

---

**12. Debater B: [Response 2]**
Claim 1: Stellar rotation provides outward pressure that can help stabilize stars against collapse.
Claim 2: Considering the effect of rotation extends SMS lifetimes ([T. B. et al.]). Including it in the SMS model will reduce the number of SMS required to produce LRDs to acceptable numbers.
- "Acceptable numbers" is framing that implies the previous objection is now resolved — without actually quantifying what "acceptable" means. Otherwise, both claims are substantive and technical with a citation.
- Rating: **2**

---

**13. Debater A: [Argument 3]**
Claim 1: D. N. et al. do not actually provide an estimate for the expected lifespan of the hypothesized LRD-like phase in SMS with stellar rotation.
Claim 2: The claim therefore lacks support: the modeling of SMS (rotation, radiative transfer, and related effects) is not yet rigorous enough to be conclusive.
- "Do not actually provide" uses "actually" for mild emphasis/rhetorical effect. "Lacks support" and "not yet rigorous enough to be conclusive" are substantive methodological critiques but also function as rhetorical dismissals. "Therefore" creates a logical-looking chain that moves from a gap in one paper to a broad characterization of the entire modeling approach as insufficiently rigorous — a somewhat overreaching rhetorical leap.
- Rating: **2**

---

**14. Debater B: [Response 3]**
Claim 1: The SMS model already fits the key observed properties of LRDs across multiple cosmic epochs, supplemented by phenomenological approximations.
Claim 2: Even a fuller treatment of radiative transfer or line strength will not appreciably alter the framework's overall fit.
- "Already fits the key observed properties" uses "already" for rhetorical effect, implying the model is well-established. "Will not appreciably alter" is a confident prediction dismissing the opponent's concern without rigorous justification — an assertion that functions rhetorically. "Overall fit" is vague. Claim 2 in particular is confident assertion masquerading as substantive rebuttal.
- Rating: **3**
