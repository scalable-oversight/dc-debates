**2. Debater B: [Argument 1] (Left)**
Claim 1: "A star continually radiates energy away from its surface in the form of photons."
Claim 2: "The temperature of the system does not increase due to the amount of energy actively being taken away from it."

Claim 1 is competently expressed and flows reasonably well. Claim 2 is somewhat awkward — "due to the amount of energy actively being taken away from it" is a clunky construction; "actively being taken away" is wordy and slightly informal. The phrase "the system" shifts register somewhat abruptly. Overall plain and slightly unpolished.

**3. Debater A: [Response 1] (Left)**
Claim 1: "The temperature of a star is proportional to the kinetic energy of its constituent particles via the equipartition theorem."
Claim 2: "The kinetic energy of the constituent particles of the star increases, as it evolves, on the main sequence."

Both claims are competently structured. "Constituent particles" appears in both, which creates slight repetitiveness. The comma placement in Claim 2 ("increases, as it evolves, on the main sequence") is functional but creates a slightly awkward rhythm. Decent but unremarkable expression.
**Rating: 3**

**4. Debater B: [Argument 2] (Left)**
Claim 1: "By the first law of thermodynamics, energy lost via radiation must come from the system's internal energy or work done on the system."
Claim 2: "Since no external work is being done on the system, the internal energy must decrease, which comes from the kinetic energy of the constituent particles."

Claim 1 is well-structured with a clean logical flow. Claim 2 has a slightly awkward construction: "the internal energy must decrease, which comes from the kinetic energy" — the relative clause "which comes from" attaches ambiguously (does the decrease come from KE, or the internal energy itself?). Functional but not polished.
**Rating: 3**

**5. Debater A: [Response 2] (Left)**
Claim 1: "When a self-gravitating system contracts, its gravitational potential energy decreases."
Claim 2: "Gravity does work on the system and the released gravitational potential energy more than offsets the radiated energy. The surplus increases the kinetic energy of the particles."

Claim 1 is clean and precise. Claim 2 is fairly well-expressed; "more than offsets" is an apt and effective phrase. "The surplus increases the kinetic energy" is crisp and logical. Two short sentences create a nice rhythmic effect. This is among the better-expressed turns.
**Rating: 4**

**6. Debater B: [Argument 3] (Left)**

Decent overall expression.

**9. Debater A: [Argument 1] (Right)**
Claim 1: "Star cores contract as they evolve on the main sequence."
Claim 2: "Gravity gets stronger as the star contracts. If the internal temperature also decreases, the star would collapse at a thermal timescale."

Claim 1 is plain but clear. "Collapse at a thermal timescale" is a somewhat awkward phrase. The conditional logic is clear but expression is unpolished.

**10. Debater B: [Response 1] (Right)**
Claim 1: "Nuclear fusion generates the radiation pressure and thermal pressure that support the star against gravitational collapse."
Claim 2: "Decreasing outward pressure as the star runs out of nuclear fuel causes the star's contraction. This does not cause collapse."

Claim 1 is well-constructed and flows naturally. Claim 2 has an awkward opening — "Decreasing outward pressure as the star runs out of nuclear fuel" is a dense noun phrase as a sentence subject, and "causes the star's contraction" then feels slightly abrupt. The final short sentence "This does not cause collapse" is blunt — while direct, it feels slightly flat in isolation.
**Rating: 3**

**11. Debater A: [Argument 2] (Right)**
Claim 1: "Nuclear fusion in the star's core converts hydrogen into helium during the main sequence."
Claim 2: "This in turn reduces the number of particles and hence the thermal pressure in the core. If the temperature does not increase to compensate, hydrostatic equilibrium cannot be maintained."

Claim 1 is clear and clean. Claim 2 is well-structured: "in turn," "hence," and the conditional construction all contribute to a logical, flowing argument. "Hydrostatic equilibrium cannot be maintained" is precise and appropriate. This is one of the more fluent turns.
**Rating: 4**

**12. Debater B: [Response 2] (Right)**
Claim 1: "Over time, the core gradually gets denser as hydrogen is steadily converted into helium."
Claim 2: "The higher density enables the core to withstand more inward pressure from the outer layers of the star. This is why temperature does not need to increase to maintain equilibrium."

Claim 1 uses "gradually" and "steadily" in close proximity, which is slightly redundant stylistically. Claim 2 is reasonably clear; "enables the core to withstand" is a decent construction. "This is why" is a functional connector but not particularly elegant. Overall competent but unexceptional.
**Rating: 3**

**13. Debater A: [Argument 3] (Right)**
Claim 1: "The self-gravity of an object increases as it grows denser at constant mass."
Claim 2: "The core's own inward pull and the pressure from the outer layers of the star grow stronger with contraction; the temperature must in turn increase to counteract them and prevent collapse."

Claim 1 is precise and well-phrased. Claim 2 is notably well-constructed: the semicolon neatly separates cause and consequence, "inward pull" is vivid, and "counteract them and prevent collapse" provides a strong, clear conclusion. This is one of the more rhetorically effective turns.
**Rating: 4**
