**2. Debater B: [Argument 1] (Left)**
Claim 1: "A ball on a string spins faster when its radius is reduced, and the same principle applies as the car transitions to a tighter radius."

Claim 1 uses a familiar analogy cleanly introduced, with "transitions to a tighter radius" being reasonably apt. Claim 2 is compact and uses technical vocabulary ("radially inward," "tangentially") with confidence. Both claims are expressed clearly. Nothing is awkward, though nothing is particularly polished or memorable either.

**Rating: 3**

**3. Debater A: [Response 1] (Left)**
Claim 1: "The string does work because, as the ball spirals inward, its velocity has a radial component aligned with the tension."
Claim 2: "The fixed frictionless track exerts only a normal constraint force perpendicular to the car's velocity, so unlike the string it does no work."

Claim 1 is precise and well-constructed—"spirals inward" is vivid, and "aligned with the tension" is apt. Claim 2 builds on this neatly with the contrast "unlike the string," which is an effective rhetorical move. The turn as a whole is coherent and flows well.

**Rating: 4**

**4. Debater B: [Argument 2] (Left)**

"At every instant, the force and velocity rotate together" is a nicely expressed idea—the image of simultaneous rotation is elegant. "Perpendicularity is maintained even as curvature changes" has a clean, assertive quality. The brevity throughout is somewhat stark, but the expression is crisp.

**9. Debater A: [Argument 1] (Right)**
