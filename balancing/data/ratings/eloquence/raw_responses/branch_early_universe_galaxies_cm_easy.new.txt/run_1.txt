**2. Debater B: [Argument 1] (Left)**

Claim 1 has a slightly redundant intensifier pair ("strongly and reliably") and "broadly speaking" is inserted reasonably but the overall sentence is slightly clunky with the trailing "across the sky." Claim 2 is clear and direct but plain. The parenthetical "(high-redshift)" is a useful clarification but adds a slight roughness. Functional but not polished.

**3. Debater A: [Response 1] (Left)**

Claim 1 introduces "top-heavy mix of masses" then immediately clarifies with "a top-heavy IMF" — the repetition of "top-heavy" and "mix" is slightly inelegant, and the parenthetical feels like a mid-sentence correction. Claim 2 is grammatically straightforward but "over its fit presented in S. S. M. et al." is a somewhat awkward comparative construction. The turn is competent but a bit rough in places.

**4. Debater B: [Argument 2] (Left)**
Claim 1: "The standard IMF is based on dense empirical data from the Milky Way and neighbouring galaxies."

Claim 2 is more awkward — "a different IMF than we've empirically observed" is a somewhat clunky construction, and the interjected clause "for a whole population of stars" breaks the sentence flow noticeably. "Is not justified by the data available" with "available" at the end feels like an afterthought. Slightly below average in expression.

**5. Debater A: [Response 2] (Left)**

Claim 1 is clean and direct — a well-formed negative statement that is easy to parse. Claim 2 is also well-constructed, with a clear causal chain ("lower metallicity… will result in… more massive stars"). "Systematically lower metallicity" is a competent technical phrase. Both claims are functional and readable, if not particularly elegant. A slight step up from the previous turns.
