**2. Debater B: [Argument 1] (Left)**
Claim 1: "LRD spectra show a Balmer break (a step at 365 nm) matching galaxy-only or dust-reddened models."
Claim 2: "Prominent Balmer breaks can be cleanly explained by photospheric emission in supermassive stars."

Claim 1 is clear and technically precise; the parenthetical "(a step at 365 nm)" is a helpful, well-placed gloss. Claim 2 uses "cleanly explained," which is a somewhat effective adverb choice. Both sentences are grammatically sound and reasonably fluent. Nothing especially polished but no awkwardness either.

**Rating: 3**

**3. Debater A: [Response 1] (Left)**
Claim 1: "Balmer breaks in high-redshift AGN are associated with absorption from dense gas in the line of sight."
Claim 2: "D. N. et al's use of speculative theories like supermassive stars to explain phenomena that can be defined by known physics may not be needed."

Claim 1 is concise and clear. Claim 2 is slightly awkward—"phenomena that can be defined by known physics" is a clunky construction, and "may not be needed" is a weak, hedged ending. The phrase "speculative theories" is effective as a rhetorical framing, but overall the second claim undermines the turn's fluency.

**Rating: 2**

**4. Debater B: [Argument 2] (Left)**
Claim 1: "Postulating something other than typical AGN is warranted: LRDs are X-ray weak while AGN are typically strong in X-rays."
Claim 2: "LRDs also show, on the whole, significantly weaker variability than typical observed AGN."

Claim 1 opens with a confident assertion followed by a colon-introduced justification—a rhetorically effective structure. "Postulating something other than typical AGN is warranted" has good formal register. Claim 2 uses "on the whole" as a hedging qualifier that fits naturally. Both claims are well-expressed and reasonably fluent.

**Rating: 3**

**5. Debater A: [Response 2] (Left)**
Claim 1: "Typical AGN accrete material below the Eddington limit, and super-Eddington accretion has been observed in highly active AGN."
Claim 2: "[K. I. et al.] show that super-Eddington accretion can produce X-ray weak AGN with weak variability. This aligns with observations of LRDS."

Both claims are clear and structured. The two-sentence structure of Claim 2 works adequately. "This aligns with observations of LRDS" is a workmanlike concluding phrase. "LRDS" (capitalization inconsistency with "LRDs" used elsewhere) is a minor blemish. Nothing ambitious rhetorically, but competent.

**Rating: 3**

**6. Debater B: [Argument 3] (Left)**

Claim 1 is clear but somewhat meandering—"events like a Tidal Disruption Event" is an awkward construction. Claim 2 has a grammatical clunkiness: "Your and Z. Z. et al.'s use" is an ungainly possessive, and "is not sound" is an abrupt ending. The overall impression is functional but unpolished.

**7. Debater A: [Response 3] (Left)**
Claim 1: "We know that super-Eddington accretion was much more prevalent in the early universe than the late universe."
Claim 2: "The observed data for LRDs supports the super-Eddington accretion hypothesis. The inability to perform additional tests does not provide evidence against the hypothesis."

Claim 1 is direct and clear. Claim 2 makes a logical rhetorical move—arguing from the absence of disconfirming evidence—and does so in two short, clean sentences. "The inability to perform additional tests does not provide evidence against the hypothesis" is a well-formed epistemic statement. Reasonably polished.

**Rating: 3**

**10. Debater B: [Response 1] (Right)**
Claim 1: "It is almost certain that supermassive stars existed in the early universe, made up of pure hydrogen and helium."

Claim 1 is clear, though "made up of pure hydrogen and helium" as a trailing participial phrase is slightly awkward. Claim 2 is functional and clear but "has an LRD-like spectrum for around 1000 years" is plain. "A valid candidate" is a reasonable concluding phrase. Generally adequate.

**11. Debater A: [Argument 2] (Right)**
Claim 1: "Direct collapse of supermassive stars is theorized to generate black hole seeds for SMBH."
Claim 2: "If the SMS model only resembles LRDs for 1000 years, the number of SMS required would be too high compared to observed SMBHs."

Claim 1 is concise and clear—"generate black hole seeds" is a vivid, technical phrase used aptly. Claim 2 presents a conditional argument cleanly. "The number of SMS required would be too high compared to observed SMBHs" is slightly informal but clear. Together, these are reasonably well-expressed.

**Rating: 3**

**12. Debater B: [Response 2] (Right)**
Claim 1: "Stellar rotation provides outward pressure that can help stabilize stars against collapse."
Claim 2: "Considering the effect of rotation extends SMS lifetimes ([T. B. et al.]). Including it in the SMS model will reduce the number of SMS required to produce LRDs to acceptable numbers."

Claim 1 is clear and well-phrased. Claim 2 is split into two sentences; the first is a dangling-feeling fragment ("Considering the effect of rotation extends SMS lifetimes"), and the second is clearer but ends with the slightly flat "to acceptable numbers." The structural awkwardness of Claim 2 drags the turn down.

**Rating: 2**

**13. Debater A: [Argument 3] (Right)**

**14. Debater B: [Response 3] (Right)**
