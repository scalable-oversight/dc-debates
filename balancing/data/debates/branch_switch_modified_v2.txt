================================================================================
KEY QUESTION
================================================================================

Key Question: A train car is freely coasting around a large circular track. It is then switched to a smaller circular track. How does its speed change? Assume no friction.

================================================================================
DEFINITIONS
================================================================================

Point mass: An idealized object with mass but no spatial extent, used to simplify analysis by ignoring rotation and internal structure.

Rigid body: An idealized solid object in which the distance between every pair of points remains constant, so it can rotate as well as translate.

Tangent: A straight line that touches a curve at exactly one point and points in the direction the curve is heading at that point.

Radially: In the direction pointing toward or away from the center of a circular path.

Tangentially: In the direction along the instantaneous velocity of an object moving on a curved path, i.e. perpendicular to the radial direction.

Normal: Perpendicular to a surface or line at a given point.

Constraint force: A force exerted by a surface, track, or other boundary that restricts an object's motion to a particular path.

Kinetic energy (KE): The energy an object possesses due to its motion, equal to ½mv² for an object of mass m moving at speed v.

DEBATE — LEFT SIDE OF TABLE  (with reactions from the other debater)
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Opening]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The speed stays the same when we model the car as a point mass. It decreases slightly when modeled as a rigid body.

   ⮕ ✘ Debater B objects

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Argument 1]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: A ball on a string spins faster when its radius is reduced, and the same principle applies as the car transitions to a tighter radius.
   → ✘ Debater A objects

Claim 2: The connecting track provides a radially inward force that accelerates the car tangentially.
   → ✘ Debater A objects

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Response 1]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: The string does work because, as the ball spirals inward, its velocity has a radial component aligned with the tension.
   → ✔ Debater B fully agrees

Claim 2: The fixed frictionless track exerts only a normal constraint force perpendicular to the car's velocity, so unlike the string it does no work.
   → ✘ Debater B objects

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Argument 2]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: Curvature changes continuously along the connecting arc, causing the direction of the constraint force to rotate.
   → ✔ Debater A fully agrees

Claim 2: This rotation means the force cannot remain perfectly perpendicular to velocity.
   → ✘ Debater A objects

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Response 2]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: The car's velocity is tangent to the track at every point. The constraint force is normal to the track, hence perpendicular to velocity.
   → ✘ Debater B objects

Claim 2: At every instant, the force and velocity rotate together. Perpendicularity is maintained even as curvature changes.
   → ✘ Debater B objects

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Argument 3]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: On a changing-curvature path, the constraint force must adjust in both magnitude and direction.
   → ✔ 🚩 Debater A agrees but thinks this omits key info

Claim 2: The centripetal acceleration behaves differently on the connecting track than it does on either circular section.
   → ✔ 🚩 Debater A agrees but thinks this omits key info

   ⮕ ✘ Debater A objects to the idea that these claims refute their argument

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Response 3]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: The force adjusts direction to maintain perpendicularity; it rotates to stay normal as velocity rotates to stay tangent.
   → ✘ Debater B objects

Claim 2: Centripetal force remains perpendicular to the velocity throughout, regardless of how its magnitude varies.
   → ✔ 🚩 Debater B agrees but thinks this omits key info

   ⮕ ✘ Debater B objects to the idea that these claims refute their argument

DEBATE — RIGHT SIDE OF TABLE  (with reactions from the other debater)
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Opening]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The speed increases if we model the car as a point mass. It also increases if we model it as a rigid body.

   ⮕ ✘ Debater A objects

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Argument 1]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: On a frictionless track, the rails' constraint force does no work, so kinetic energy (KE) is conserved and speed cannot increase.
   → ✘ Debater B objects

Claim 2: There is simply nothing on a fixed frictionless track that can add energy to the car.
   → ✘ Debater B objects

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Response 1]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: If the rails truly do no work, a point-mass car will not lose speed on the smaller track.
   → ✔ Debater A fully agrees

Claim 2: Yet if kinetic energy is conserved while the rails do no work, a rigid body on a frictionless track does not slow down, contradicting Debater A's Opening.
   → ✘ Debater A objects

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Argument 2]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: A rigid body has translational and rotational kinetic energy; a point mass has only translational.
   → ✔ Debater B fully agrees

Claim 2: On a tighter track, rotational kinetic energy increases at the expense of translational kinetic energy.
   → ✘ Debater B objects

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Response 2]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: This holds only when the body is mechanically constrained to yaw with the track curvature (rigid link between axles and body), which has not been established.
   → ✘ Debater A objects

Claim 2: Without that constraint, total KE stays translational and the speed does not fall.
   → ✘ Debater A objects

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater A: [Argument 3]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: Both axles are constrained to the rails, so the body's orientation is pinned by the chord between the two axle positions.
   → ✔ 🚩 Debater B agrees but thinks this omits key info

Claim 2: On a curved section, this geometry causes the body to yaw as it advances.
   → ✔ 🚩 Debater B agrees but thinks this omits key info

   ⮕ ✘ Debater B objects to the idea that these claims refute their argument

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Debater B: [Response 3]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claim 1: A car whose axles are rigidly fixed parallel to its body cannot roll without slipping on a curved track.
   → ✔ 🚩 Debater A agrees but thinks this omits key info

Claim 2: The curvature differs at the front and rear contact points on the connecting track, with consequences for yaw and speed.
   → ✘ Debater A objects

   ⮕ ✘ Debater A objects to the idea that these claims refute their argument
